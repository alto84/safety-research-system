## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Antibody-drug conjugate (ADC)-induced interstitial lung disease (ILD) represents a significant clinical challenge with mortality rates exceeding 26% overall and 65% for acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) presentations. This comprehensive literature review examines treatment approaches, management protocols, and therapeutic interventions for ADC-associated ILD based on recent evidence from 2023-2025. Key findings indicate that corticosteroids remain the cornerstone of treatment, with dose and duration varying by toxicity grade. However, steroid-refractory cases require additional immunosuppressive agents, and ADC rechallenge decisions must be individualized based on severity and recovery parameters. Multidisciplinary team coordination and early detection through proactive monitoring are essential for optimizing outcomes.

---

## 1. PHARMACOLOGICAL INTERVENTIONS

### 1.1 Corticosteroids (First-Line Treatment)

Corticosteroids represent the mainstay of treatment for ADC-induced ILD/pneumonitis, with dosing protocols stratified by toxicity grade.

#### **Grade 1 (Asymptomatic) Pneumonitis**

**Dosing Regimen:**
- Initial dose: ≥0.5 mg/kg/day prednisolone or equivalent
- Duration: Until improvement, followed by gradual taper over ≥4 weeks
- Alternative approach: Consider observation with close monitoring in select cases

**Evidence Base:**
Multidisciplinary clinical guidance on trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd) recommends early corticosteroid intervention even for asymptomatic grade 1 events to prevent progression. The Canadian Product Monograph specifies >0.5 mg/kg/day prednisolone until improvement with a gradual taper over 4 weeks or longer.

**Efficacy Data:**
In pooled analyses of T-DXd studies, most grade 1 cases (83.1%) treated with corticosteroids were considered responsive to treatment. The median time to radiographic improvement was shorter in patients treated with steroids (29 days) compared to those not treated (82 days).

**Rechallenge Safety:**
Rechallenge with T-DXd after grade 1 ILD appears safe in real-world populations. Of 47 patients rechallenged after grade 1 events, only 3 (6.4%) experienced recurrent ILD/pneumonitis. Approximately 18% of patients received retreatment for more than a year, and 67% of retreated patients did not experience recurrent ILD.

**Reference:** Powell CA, et al. Multidisciplinary clinical guidance on trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd)–related interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis. Cancer Treat Rev. 2022;104:102351. DOI: 10.1016/j.ctrv.2022.102351

---

#### **Grade 2 (Symptomatic, Mild-Moderate) Pneumonitis**

**Dosing Regimen:**
- Initial dose: ≥1 mg/kg/day prednisone or equivalent
- Duration: Minimum 14 days, continue until complete resolution of clinical and radiographic findings
- Taper: Gradual reduction over ≥4 weeks following resolution
- ADC Management: Permanent discontinuation of T-DXd and similar high-risk ADCs

**Evidence Base:**
Multiple guidelines consistently recommend permanent discontinuation of the offending ADC for grade 2 or higher symptomatic pneumonitis, combined with systemic corticosteroids at 1 mg/kg/day for at least 14 days.

**Efficacy and Outcomes:**
The overall proportion of recovery from ADC-related ILD/pneumonitis (including recovered, recovered with sequelae, and recovering) was 76.9%, with a median time to recovery of 83.5 days (IQR: 42.25–143.75 days). Most corticosteroid-treated cases (59/71, 83.1%) showed treatment responsiveness.

**Clinical Context:**
In real-world practice, 19 patients with grade 2 ILD were rechallenged outside guidelines. These patients continued T-DXd for a median of 91 days after rechallenge, with three developing recurrent ILD of higher grade. However, there were no grade 5 (fatal) cases of recurrent ILD after rechallenge, suggesting some flexibility in select cases with careful monitoring.

**Reference:** Curigliano G, et al. Managing the Risk of Lung Toxicity with Trastuzumab Deruxtecan (T-DXd): A Canadian Perspective. Curr Oncol. 2023;30(9):8081-8099. DOI: 10.3390/curroncol30090582

---

#### **Grade 3-4 (Severe/Life-Threatening) Pneumonitis**

**Dosing Regimen:**
- Pulse therapy: Methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg/day IV for 3 days
- Maintenance: ≥1 mg/kg/day prednisone or equivalent
- Duration: ≥14 days or until complete resolution of clinical and chest CT findings
- Taper: Gradual reduction over ≥4 weeks
- ADC Management: Permanent discontinuation (absolute contraindication to rechallenge)

**Evidence Base:**
High-dose empirical methylprednisolone pulse therapy represents the standard of care for severe ADC-induced pneumonitis. This approach mirrors treatment protocols for other severe drug-induced lung injuries and immune-mediated pneumonitis.

**ICU Management Considerations:**
Grade 3 disease (severe) includes symptoms that worsen quality of life and affect daily living with possible need for oxygen therapy. Grade 4 (very severe, life-threatening, or disabling) includes severe, disabling symptoms leading to hospitalization and possibly mechanical ventilatory support.

**Mortality Data:**
Short- and long-term mortality is exceptionally high in patients with ILD requiring invasive mechanical ventilation (30-day mortality: 78%; 6-month mortality: 96%). ARDS associated with ADC-related ILD carries the highest mortality rate at 65.0%.

**Supportive Care:**
Management includes oxygen therapy, non-invasive ventilation when appropriate, and mechanical ventilation in life-threatening cases. However, decisions about invasive ventilation should consider the patient's cancer prognosis and goals of care.

**Reference:** Powell CA, et al. Multidisciplinary clinical guidance on trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd)–related interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis. Cancer Treat Rev. 2022;104:102351.

---

#### **Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia (PJP) Prophylaxis**

**Recommendation:**
For patients receiving high-dose systemic corticosteroids (≥20 mg prednisone equivalent for ≥1 month), particularly pulse steroids, PJP prophylaxis with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole should be considered.

**Rationale:**
Prolonged high-dose corticosteroid therapy for ADC-induced pneumonitis creates significant immunosuppression risk. PJP prophylaxis helps prevent opportunistic infections during treatment.

**Dosing:**
Standard prophylaxis regimens include trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole single-strength (80/400 mg) daily or double-strength (160/800 mg) three times weekly.

**Reference:** Hurvitz SA, et al. Clinical Guidance on the Monitoring and Management of Trastuzumab Deruxtecan (T-DXd)-Related Adverse Events. Drug Saf. 2023;46:979-995. DOI: 10.1007/s40264-023-01328-x

### 1.2 Steroid-Refractory Disease and Alternative Immunosuppressants

#### **Clinical Scenario and Definition**

Steroid-refractory ADC-induced ILD is defined as lack of clinical and/or radiographic improvement after 3-5 days of appropriate corticosteroid therapy, or progressive deterioration despite treatment.

#### **Second-Line Immunosuppressive Agents**

**Infliximab (TNF-α Inhibitor):**
- Mechanism: Blocks tumor necrosis factor-alpha, reducing inflammatory cascade
- Dosing: Typically 5 mg/kg IV infusion
- Evidence: Used for corticosteroid-refractory immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) pneumonitis; extrapolated to ADC-induced ILD
- Indication: Consider if glucocorticoids are ineffective after 3-5 days

**Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF):**
- Mechanism: Inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase, reducing T and B lymphocyte proliferation
- Evidence base: Established efficacy in connective tissue disease-associated ILD and myositis-related ILD
- Tolerability: 82% of patients continued MMF for at least 6 months in ILD populations
- ADC Context: Can be considered for steroid-sparing or steroid-refractory cases

**Cyclophosphamide:**
- Mechanism: Alkylating agent with potent immunosuppressive effects
- Evidence: Second-line treatment in steroid-refractory ILD-associated acute respiratory failure
- Dosing: Intravenous pulse therapy
- Safety considerations: Significant toxicity profile including bone marrow suppression and bladder toxicity

**Azathioprine (AZA):**
- Mechanism: Purine synthesis inhibitor
- Evidence: Described efficacy in myositis-related ILD with improved FVC % predicted and lower prednisone dose
- Tolerability: Lower continuation rate (40% at 6 months) compared to MMF due to adverse drug reactions
- ADC Context: Limited specific evidence; generally less preferred than MMF

**Intravenous Immunoglobulin (IVIG):**
- Mechanism: Multiple immunomodulatory effects including anti-inflammatory actions
- Evidence: Anecdotal reports in refractory drug-induced pneumonitis
- Dosing: Typically 2 g/kg divided over 2-5 days

**Reference:** Zhang X, et al. Advancements in research and clinical management of interstitial lung injury associated with ADC drugs administration in breast cancer. Discov Oncol. 2024;15:41. DOI: 10.1007/s12672-024-01705-7

---

#### **Chronic Suppressive Corticosteroid Therapy**

**Rationale:**
Chronic "suppressive" corticosteroid therapies could be considered for ADC-induced pneumonitis, as ADCs do not necessarily rely on antitumor immunity for their mechanisms of action (unlike immune checkpoint inhibitors).

**Approach:**
- Low-dose maintenance prednisone (5-10 mg daily) for prolonged periods
- May allow cancer treatment continuation with alternative ADC or non-ADC therapy
- Must balance benefit against corticosteroid adverse effects (osteoporosis, hyperglycemia, infection risk, myopathy)

**Evidence Limitations:**
This approach is theoretical and based on mechanistic reasoning rather than clinical trial data. No systematic studies have evaluated chronic corticosteroid suppression for ongoing ADC therapy continuation.

---

### 1.3 Antifibrotic Agents (Nintedanib, Pirfenidone)

#### **Background and Mechanism**

**Nintedanib:**
- Mechanism: Potent kinase inhibitor blocking platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), and fibroblast growth factor (FGF) receptors
- FDA approval: Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) and progressive fibrosing ILD

**Pirfenidone:**
- Mechanism: Unclear; proposed anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, and antifibrotic effects
- FDA approval: IPF and progressive fibrosing ILD

#### **Evidence in ADC-Induced ILD**

**No Specific Data:**
Literature search found no published studies specifically evaluating nintedanib or pirfenidone for ADC-induced ILD. These agents are approved for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis and progressive fibrosing ILD but have not been studied in drug-induced pneumonitis.

**Theoretical Application:**
In cases where ADC-induced ILD progresses to chronic fibrotic changes despite corticosteroid treatment, antifibrotic therapy could theoretically slow progression. However, this remains speculative.

**Evidence in Other Drug-Induced ILD:**
Nintedanib was recently approved for progressive fibrotic hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP), demonstrating efficacy in slowing FVC decline. This suggests potential utility in other fibrosing ILD forms, but extrapolation to ADC-induced ILD requires caution.

**Reference:** Flaherty KR, et al. Nintedanib in Progressive Fibrosing Interstitial Lung Diseases. N Engl J Med. 2019;381:1718-1727. (Note: General reference on antifibrotics, not ADC-specific)

### 1.4 Supportive Therapies

#### **Oxygen Supplementation**

**Indications:**
- Hypoxemia (SpO2 <90% or PaO2 <60 mmHg)
- Symptomatic dyspnea with exertional desaturation
- Grade 2 or higher pneumonitis with respiratory compromise

**Delivery Methods:**
- Nasal cannula (low-flow oxygen)
- High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) for higher oxygen requirements
- Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (NIPPV/BiPAP) for respiratory distress
- Mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure (poor prognosis)

**Evidence:**
Emergency treatment of ILD includes oxygenation and ventilatory support for patients who develop respiratory failure. Oxygen therapy is a fundamental supportive measure across all grades of symptomatic pneumonitis.

---

#### **Bronchodilators**

**Limited Evidence:**
Literature search found no specific evidence supporting routine bronchodilator use in ADC-induced pneumonitis. Bronchodilators target airway smooth muscle constriction and are most effective in obstructive airway diseases (asthma, COPD).

**Potential Role:**
Some patients with pneumonitis may have concomitant bronchospasm or pre-existing obstructive lung disease. In such cases, bronchodilators may provide symptomatic relief but do not address the underlying inflammatory process.

---

#### **Pulmonary Rehabilitation**

After recovery from acute pneumonitis, pulmonary rehabilitation may help restore functional capacity, particularly in patients with prolonged illness or residual pulmonary impairment.

---

### 1.5 Novel and Experimental Treatments

#### **Liquid Biopsy for Early Detection**

**Circulating Cell-Free DNA (cfDNA):**
Detection of lung-derived circulating cfDNA using tissue-specific methylation analysis was associated with ADC-induced ILD in patients treated with T-DXd. This liquid biopsy approach may serve as a cost-effective detection and monitoring tool for ADC-induced ILD, potentially enabling earlier intervention.

**Reference:** Nakamura Y, et al. Detection of antibody–drug conjugate-induced interstitial lung disease using circulating cell-free DNA. ESMO Open. 2024;9:103654. DOI: 10.1016/j.esmoop.2024.103654

## 2. ADC-SPECIFIC MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES

### 2.1 Dose Modification Protocols

#### **Trastuzumab Deruxtecan (T-DXd / Enhertu)**

**Grade 1 ILD - Resolution ≤28 Days:**
- Interrupt T-DXd until resolved to Grade 0 (normalization of CT abnormalities)
- Resume at same dose (5.4 mg/kg for breast/gastric cancer)

**Grade 1 ILD - Resolution >28 Days:**
- Interrupt T-DXd until resolved to Grade 0
- Reduce dose by one level:
  - First occurrence: 4.4 mg/kg (from 5.4 mg/kg)
  - Second occurrence: 3.2 mg/kg (from 4.4 mg/kg)

**Grade 2 or Higher ILD:**
- Permanent discontinuation (no rechallenge permitted)

**Reference:** ENHERTU (fam-trastuzumab deruxtecan-nxki) Prescribing Information. Daiichi Sankyo/AstraZeneca. 2024.

---

#### **Datopotamab Deruxtecan (Dato-DXd / Datopotamab)**

**Incidence:**
Adjudicated drug-related ILD/pneumonitis grade ≥3 occurred in 3.4% of patients in the TROPION-Lung01 trial. Among 26 patients (8.8%) who had adjudicated drug-related ILD/pneumonitis: 2 (0.7%) grade 1, 13 (4.4%) grade 2, 3 (1.0%) grade 3, 1 (0.3%) grade 4, and 7 (2.4%) grade 5 events.

**Dose Reduction Schedule:**
- Starting dose: 6.0 mg/kg
- First reduction: 4.0 mg/kg
- Second reduction: 3.0 mg/kg

**Management Approach:**
Grade-specific management similar to T-DXd, with dose modifications based on severity and recovery time.

**Reference:** Goto K, et al. Datopotamab Deruxtecan Versus Docetaxel for Previously Treated Advanced or Metastatic Non–Small Cell Lung Cancer. J Clin Oncol. 2024;42:3998-4007. DOI: 10.1200/JCO-24-01544

---

#### **Enfortumab Vedotin (Padcev)**

**Incidence:**
When combined with pembrolizumab: 10% any grade, 4% grade 3-4 (median time to onset: 4 months, range 0.3-26 months). As single agent: 3% any grade, 0.8% grade 3-4.

**Management:**
- Grade 2: Withhold and consider dose reduction
- Grade 3-4: Permanent discontinuation

**Reference:** PADCEV (enfortumab vedotin-ejfv) Prescribing Information. Astellas/Pfizer. 2024.

---

#### **Mirvetuximab Soravtansine (Elahere)**

**Incidence:**
10% pneumonitis overall; 1% grade 3, 0.1% grade 4. Led to permanent discontinuation in 3% of patients.

**Management:**
- Grade 1 (asymptomatic): May continue with close monitoring
- Grade 2 (persistent/recurrent): Withhold until ≤Grade 1, consider dose reduction
- Grade 3-4: Permanent discontinuation

**Reference:** ELAHERE (mirvetuximab soravtansine-gynx) Prescribing Information. ImmunoGen. 2024.

---

#### **Tisotumab Vedotin (Tivdak)**

**Incidence:**
Among 720 patients treated as single agent: 3% any grade, 0.8% grade 3-4. Four patients (0.9%) across trials experienced pneumonitis, including one fatal outcome.

**Management:**
- Grade 2 (persistent/recurrent): Withhold, consider dose reduction
- Grade 3-4: Permanent discontinuation

**Reference:** TIVDAK (tisotumab vedotin-tftv) Prescribing Information. Seagen/Pfizer. 2024.

---

#### **Sacituzumab Govitecan (Trodelvy)**

**Incidence:**
Very low incidence. In the phase 3 ASCENT trial, only one patient had reversible grade 3 pneumonitis in the context of multiple confounding factors. No sacituzumab govitecan-related deaths occurred from ILD. ILD rate <1%.

**Clinical Significance:**
Sacituzumab govitecan has a markedly lower pulmonary toxicity profile compared to topoisomerase I inhibitor-containing deruxtecan ADCs. This makes it a potential option for sequential therapy after T-DXd-induced ILD recovery.

**Reference:** Bardia A, et al. Safety analyses from the phase 3 ASCENT trial of sacituzumab govitecan in metastatic triple-negative breast cancer. NPJ Breast Cancer. 2022;8:98. DOI: 10.1038/s41523-022-00467-1

### 2.2 Treatment Interruption Criteria

**When to Interrupt ADC Therapy:**

1. **Radiographic Findings:**
   - New or worsening ground-glass opacities on CT scan
   - Bilateral consolidations
   - Interstitial changes consistent with ILD

2. **Clinical Symptoms:**
   - New or worsening dyspnea
   - New or worsening cough
   - Hypoxia (SpO2 <90%)
   - Fever without alternative explanation

3. **Grade 1 (Asymptomatic) Detection:**
   - Any radiographic ILD finding, even without symptoms
   - Proactive monitoring protocols increasingly detect asymptomatic grade 1 disease

**Time to Resolution Monitoring:**
Follow-up thoracic imaging within 28 days of onset determines dose resumption strategy. The 28-day threshold is critical for T-DXd rechallenge decisions.

---

### 2.3 Rechallenge Safety and Outcomes

#### **Grade 1 Rechallenge Data (T-DXd)**

**Evidence from 2025 ASCO Pooled Analysis:**
Rechallenge with T-DXd after grade 1 ILD appeared safe in a diverse real-world population. Key findings:

- 47 patients rechallenged after grade 1 events
- Only 3 (6.4%) had recurrence of ILD/pneumonitis
- All recurrent ILD events were low grade (≤2)
- Recurrent events were generally manageable using existing treatment guidelines
- 67% of retreated patients did not experience recurrent ILD
- Approximately 18% of patients received retreatment for more than a year

**Radiographic Resolution Timing:**
Median time to radiographic improvement was 29 days with steroid treatment vs 82 days without steroids, supporting early corticosteroid intervention even for grade 1 disease.

**Reference:** Modi S, et al. Pooled analysis of trastuzumab deruxtecan retreatment after recovery from grade 1 interstitial lung disease/pneumonitis. Ann Oncol. 2025 (presented ASCO 2025).

---

#### **Grade 2 Rechallenge (Off-Label/Outside Guidelines)**

**Real-World Experience:**
In one real-world study, 19 patients with grade 2 ILD were rechallenged outside guideline recommendations. Patients continued T-DXd for a median of 91 days after rechallenge. Three patients developed recurrent ILD of higher grade, but there were no grade 5 (fatal) cases of recurrent ILD after rechallenge.

**Clinical Interpretation:**
While current guidelines mandate permanent discontinuation for grade 2 or higher events, some clinicians consider rechallenge in select cases with:
- Complete radiographic and clinical resolution
- Strong clinical benefit from prior ADC therapy
- Limited alternative treatment options
- Intensive monitoring capability

**Caution:**
This approach remains controversial and outside current guideline recommendations. Risk-benefit assessment must be individualized.

---

#### **Grade 3-4 Rechallenge**

**Absolute Contraindication:**
All current guidelines and expert consensus statements agree that grade 3-4 pneumonitis represents an absolute contraindication to ADC rechallenge due to unacceptably high risk of fatal recurrence.

---

### 2.4 Switch to Alternative ADCs

#### **Sequential ADC Use After ILD**

**Clinical Scenario:**
After ADC-induced ILD (particularly T-DXd), can patients safely receive alternative ADCs?

**Evidence from Multi-Institutional Study:**
All 10 patients who developed ILD in one study were diagnosed during T-DXd treatment. Four patients initially received T-DXd, developed ILD, then upon recovery subsequently received sacituzumab govitecan (SG) without further ILD events. Six patients initially received SG (no ILD), then T-DXd (developed ILD).

**Cross-Reactivity Concerns:**
ADCs with different payloads and linkers may have different pulmonary toxicity profiles:
- **Topoisomerase I inhibitors (deruxtecan platform):** Higher ILD risk
- **MMAE/MMAF (auristatins):** Lower ILD risk
- **SN-38 metabolite (sacituzumab govitecan):** Very low ILD risk

**Clinical Recommendation:**
Switching from a topoisomerase I inhibitor-containing ADC (T-DXd, dato-DXd) to a non-topoisomerase I inhibitor ADC (sacituzumab govitecan, enfortumab vedotin) after ILD recovery appears feasible but requires:
- Complete resolution of ILD (clinical and radiographic)
- Multidisciplinary discussion
- Intensive monitoring
- Patient understanding of risks

**Reference:** Abstract PO4-12-06: Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD) in Sequential Antibody Drug Conjugate (ADC) use in patients with Metastatic Breast Cancer. Cancer Res. 2024;84(9_Suppl):PO4-12-06.

---

### 2.5 Prophylactic Approaches

#### **Current Evidence**

**No Proven Prophylaxis:**
Literature search found no evidence supporting prophylactic corticosteroids or other medications to prevent ADC-induced ILD before it occurs. Prophylactic strategies have not been studied in clinical trials.

**Rationale Against Prophylaxis:**
- ILD incidence varies widely by ADC (0.4% to 28.1%)
- Prophylactic corticosteroids would expose all patients to steroid toxicity to prevent events in a minority
- Mechanism of ADC-induced ILD is not fully understood
- Early detection through monitoring may be more effective than prevention

**Focus on Early Detection:**
Current best practice emphasizes proactive monitoring and early intervention rather than prophylaxis:
- Baseline CT chest before ADC initiation
- Regular interval imaging (e.g., before each cycle or every 6-12 weeks)
- Patient education on symptoms
- Low threshold for evaluation of respiratory complaints
- Prompt intervention at grade 1 (asymptomatic) stage

## 3. CLINICAL MANAGEMENT ALGORITHMS

### 3.1 Grade-Specific Management

#### **Grade 1 (Asymptomatic) - Radiographic Changes Only**

**Definition:** Radiographic changes suggestive of ILD without symptoms or oxygen requirement.

**Management Steps:**

1. **Immediate Actions:**
   - Withhold ADC immediately
   - Confirm findings with dedicated chest CT (high-resolution if not already done)
   - Exclude alternative diagnoses (infection, malignancy, heart failure)
   - Evaluate baseline pulmonary function if not recently done

2. **Diagnostic Evaluation:**
   - Detailed history (pulmonary symptoms, exposures, infections)
   - Physical examination
   - Laboratory: CBC, CMP, inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR)
   - Infectious workup: Blood cultures, respiratory viral panel, consider COVID-19 testing
   - Consider atypical pathogen testing (Mycoplasma, Legionella)
   - Pulmonology consultation

3. **Treatment:**
   - Consider corticosteroids: ≥0.5 mg/kg/day prednisolone or equivalent
   - Alternative: Close observation without steroids (controversial)
   - PJP prophylaxis if steroids initiated

4. **Monitoring:**
   - Repeat CT scan within 28 days to assess for resolution
   - Serial symptom assessment
   - Pulse oximetry

5. **ADC Management:**
   - If resolved to Grade 0 by 28 days: May resume at same dose
   - If resolved to Grade 0 after >28 days: Resume at reduced dose
   - If progression to Grade 2+: Permanent discontinuation

**Expected Outcome:**
Most grade 1 cases resolve with treatment. Rechallenge is safe in most patients.

---

#### **Grade 2 (Symptomatic, Moderate) - New/Worsening Symptoms**

**Definition:** New or worsening respiratory symptoms (dyspnea, cough) limiting instrumental activities of daily living (ADL); oxygen not indicated.

**Management Steps:**

1. **Immediate Actions:**
   - Permanently discontinue ADC (do not rechallenge)
   - Admit to hospital or manage in outpatient setting with close follow-up based on severity
   - Comprehensive diagnostic evaluation

2. **Diagnostic Workup:**
   - High-resolution CT chest
   - Complete infectious workup including:
     - Blood cultures
     - Sputum culture and gram stain (if productive cough)
     - Respiratory viral panel
     - Atypical pathogens (Mycoplasma, Legionella urine antigen)
     - Consider PJP testing (induced sputum or BAL if high suspicion)
   - Pulmonology consultation mandatory
   - Consider bronchoscopy with BAL to exclude infection and characterize cellular profile

3. **Treatment:**
   - Systemic corticosteroids: ≥1 mg/kg/day prednisone or equivalent
   - Minimum duration: 14 days
   - Continue until complete clinical and radiographic resolution
   - Gradual taper over ≥4 weeks after resolution
   - PJP prophylaxis: Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole

4. **Monitoring:**
   - Daily assessment while symptomatic
   - Pulse oximetry
   - Repeat CT at 4-6 weeks to document improvement
   - Pulmonary function testing after recovery

5. **If No Improvement in 3-5 Days:**
   - Re-evaluate for alternative diagnoses
   - Consider bronchoscopy if not yet performed
   - Consider additional immunosuppression (infliximab, mycophenolate)

**Expected Outcome:**
Most cases respond to corticosteroids within 2-4 weeks. Median time to recovery: 83.5 days overall. ADC must be permanently discontinued.

---

#### **Grade 3 (Severe) - Oxygen Required**

**Definition:** Severe symptoms limiting self-care ADL; oxygen indicated; hospitalization required.

**Management Steps:**

1. **Immediate Actions:**
   - Hospital admission (consider ICU)
   - Permanently discontinue ADC
   - Multidisciplinary team activation (oncology, pulmonology, critical care, infectious disease, radiology)

2. **Diagnostic Evaluation:**
   - High-resolution CT chest
   - Comprehensive infectious workup:
     - Blood cultures
     - Respiratory cultures (sputum, BAL)
     - Viral testing including COVID-19, influenza, RSV
     - Fungal workup (Aspergillus galactomannan, beta-D-glucan)
     - PJP testing (induced sputum or BAL)
   - Bronchoscopy with BAL strongly recommended
   - Consider transbronchial biopsy if safe

3. **Treatment:**
   - Pulse methylprednisolone: 500-1000 mg IV daily for 3 days
   - Then prednisone ≥1 mg/kg/day
   - Duration: ≥14 days or until complete resolution
   - Gradual taper over ≥4 weeks
   - PJP prophylaxis mandatory
   - Oxygen supplementation to maintain SpO2 >90%
   - Consider high-flow nasal cannula if increasing oxygen requirements

4. **If Steroid-Refractory (No Improvement in 3-5 Days):**
   - Infliximab 5 mg/kg IV
   - Alternative: Mycophenolate mofetil, cyclophosphamide, or IVIG
   - Consider repeat bronchoscopy to exclude missed infection

5. **Monitoring:**
   - Continuous pulse oximetry
   - Daily clinical assessment
   - Frequent chest imaging
   - ICU transfer if worsening

**Expected Outcome:**
Variable. Mortality risk significant. Most patients require prolonged hospitalization. Cancer treatment often cannot be resumed.

---

#### **Grade 4 (Life-Threatening) - Ventilatory Support Indicated**

**Definition:** Life-threatening respiratory compromise; urgent intervention indicated (intubation, mechanical ventilation, or non-invasive positive pressure ventilation).

**Management Steps:**

1. **Immediate Actions:**
   - ICU admission
   - Permanently discontinue ADC
   - Respiratory support as needed:
     - Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (NIPPV)
     - High-flow nasal cannula
     - Intubation and mechanical ventilation if failing less invasive measures
   - Multidisciplinary team conference including critical care, oncology, pulmonology

2. **Diagnostic Evaluation:**
   - CT chest (if patient stable enough for transport)
   - Bronchoscopy with BAL (may need to be performed after intubation)
   - Comprehensive infectious, fungal, and viral testing
   - Consider lung biopsy only if diagnosis uncertain and would change management

3. **Treatment:**
   - Pulse methylprednisolone: 1000 mg IV daily for 3 days
   - Then prednisone ≥1 mg/kg/day
   - PJP prophylaxis
   - Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics pending culture results
   - Consider early additional immunosuppression if drug-induced etiology certain:
     - Infliximab 5 mg/kg IV
     - Mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide

4. **Mechanical Ventilation Strategy:**
   - Low tidal volume ventilation (6 mL/kg ideal body weight)
   - Lung-protective strategy similar to ARDS protocol
   - Minimize FiO2 and PEEP to prevent barotrauma

5. **Prognosis Discussion:**
   - Mortality extremely high (30-day: 78%; 6-month: 96%)
   - Goals of care discussion with patient/family essential
   - Consider palliative care consultation

**Expected Outcome:**
Poor. Very high mortality even with aggressive treatment. Many patients do not survive hospitalization. Those who survive often have significant residual pulmonary impairment.

---

#### **Grade 5 (Fatal)**

**Definition:** Death attributable to ILD/pneumonitis.

**Contributing Factors (from Literature):**
- Diffuse alveolar damage (DAD) pattern on imaging (72.7% of fatal T-DXd cases)
- ARDS presentation (65% mortality rate)
- Delayed diagnosis and treatment
- Rapid progression from lower grades
- Underlying pulmonary comorbidities

**Incidence by ADC:**
- T-DXd: 2.2% (decreased from 2.7% in early trials to 0.8% in recent trials due to improved monitoring)
- Dato-DXd: 2.4% in TROPION-Lung01
- Gemtuzumab ozogamicin: 57.14%
- Inotuzumab ozogamicin: 50.00%
- Overall ADC-related ILD: 26% mortality rate

**Prevention Strategies:**
- Proactive monitoring protocols
- Early detection at grade 1 stage
- Patient education
- Prompt treatment initiation
- Multidisciplinary management

### 3.2 ICU Management for Severe Cases

#### **Indications for ICU Admission**

- Grade 3 pneumonitis with increasing oxygen requirements
- Grade 4 pneumonitis (any patient requiring mechanical ventilation)
- Hypoxemic respiratory failure despite supplemental oxygen
- Hemodynamic instability
- Altered mental status due to hypoxemia

#### **Critical Care Management**

**Respiratory Support:**
- Oxygen supplementation (target SpO2 >90%)
- High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) for moderate hypoxemia
- Non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (NIPPV/BiPAP) for acute respiratory distress
- Mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure:
  - Low tidal volume strategy (6 mL/kg ideal body weight)
  - PEEP optimization
  - FiO2 minimization
  - Prone positioning may be considered for severe ARDS

**Pharmacological Management:**
- Pulse-dose corticosteroids: Methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV daily × 3 days
- Maintenance corticosteroids: Prednisone equivalent ≥1 mg/kg/day
- PJP prophylaxis
- Stress ulcer prophylaxis
- DVT prophylaxis

**Monitoring:**
- Continuous pulse oximetry
- Arterial blood gas monitoring
- Serial chest imaging
- Daily clinical assessment by multidisciplinary team

**Prognosis:**
Short- and long-term mortality is exceptionally high (30-day mortality: 78%; 6-month mortality: 96%) in patients with ILD requiring invasive mechanical ventilation. Goals of care discussions should occur early in ICU course.

---

### 3.3 Multidisciplinary Team Coordination

#### **Core Team Members**

**Essential Participants:**
1. **Oncologist:** Manages cancer treatment, ADC decisions, systemic therapy
2. **Pulmonologist:** Leads ILD diagnosis and respiratory management
3. **Radiologist:** Interprets imaging, distinguishes ILD patterns, monitors evolution
4. **Pathologist:** If biopsy obtained, provides histologic diagnosis

**Extended Team:**
- Infectious disease specialist (to exclude infections)
- Critical care specialist (for severe/ICU cases)
- Pharmacy (drug interactions, dosing)
- Nursing (monitoring, patient education)

#### **Multidisciplinary Discussion (MDD) Process**

**Purpose:**
Multidisciplinary input and consensus are crucial in the accurate diagnosis of drug-induced ILD. Integration of expertise from different specialists achieves a 'best fit' diagnosis and management plan.

**Discussion Components:**
1. **Clinical history:** ADC type, dose, number of cycles, timing of symptoms
2. **Imaging review:** CT patterns, differential diagnosis
3. **Laboratory data:** Inflammatory markers, infectious workup results
4. **BAL findings:** If available, cellular profile and culture results
5. **Histology:** If biopsy performed
6. **Differential diagnosis:** Exclude infection, progression of cancer, heart failure, other drug effects
7. **Management plan:** Corticosteroid dosing, ADC decision, monitoring strategy
8. **Communication:** With patient regarding prognosis and treatment plan

**Benefits:**
- Improved diagnostic accuracy
- Standardized management approach
- Better patient outcomes through coordinated care
- Educational value for team members

Full article: Clinical best practices in optimal monitoring, early diagnosis, and effective management of antibody–drug conjugate-induced interstitial lung disease or pneumonitis: a multidisciplinary team approach in Singapore. Expert Opin Drug Saf. 2023;22:131-143. DOI: 10.1080/17425255.2022.2162383

## 4. OUTCOMES AND PROGNOSIS

### 4.1 Recovery Rates by Treatment Type

#### **Corticosteroid Treatment**

**Overall Recovery Rates:**
- Overall recovery proportion (including recovered, recovered with sequelae, and recovering): 76.9%
- Median time to recovery: 83.5 days (IQR: 42.25–143.75 days)
- Corticosteroid response rate: 83.1% (59/71 treated cases considered responsive)

**Recovery by Grade:**
- Grade 1: Excellent recovery rates (>90%)
- Grade 2: Good recovery rates (70-80%)
- Grade 3-4: Variable recovery; significant mortality risk

**Time to Radiographic Improvement:**
- With steroid treatment: Median 29 days
- Without steroid treatment: Median 82 days
- Demonstrates clear benefit of early corticosteroid intervention

**Reference:** Powell CA, et al. Pooled analysis of drug-related interstitial lung disease and/or pneumonitis in nine trastuzumab deruxtecan monotherapy studies. ESMO Open. 2022;12:100558. DOI: 10.1016/j.esmoop.2022.100558

---

#### **Steroid-Refractory Disease**

**Definition:** Lack of improvement or progression despite 3-5 days of appropriate corticosteroid therapy.

**Outcomes with Second-Line Immunosuppression:**
- Limited published data specifically for ADC-induced ILD
- Extrapolated from ICI-induced pneumonitis literature:
  - Infliximab: Response rates 50-70% in steroid-refractory ICI pneumonitis
  - Mycophenolate: May stabilize lung function in fibrotic ILD
  - Cyclophosphamide: Increased FVC at 6 months in steroid-refractory ILD (IPAF study)

**Prognosis:**
Steroid-refractory disease carries worse prognosis with higher mortality rates.

---

### 4.2 Predictors of Response to Treatment

#### **Favorable Prognostic Factors**

1. **Early Detection:**
   - Grade 1 (asymptomatic) detection associated with best outcomes
   - Proactive monitoring enables early intervention

2. **Radiologic Pattern:**
   - Organizing pneumonia (OP) pattern: Best prognosis, steroid-responsive
   - Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP) pattern: Good prognosis

3. **Prompt Treatment Initiation:**
   - Early corticosteroid therapy improves outcomes
   - Delayed treatment allows progression to higher grades

4. **Lower Toxicity Grade:**
   - Grade 1-2: Excellent recovery rates
   - Grade 3: Variable
   - Grade 4: Poor prognosis

---

#### **Adverse Prognostic Factors**

1. **Radiologic Pattern:**
   - Diffuse alveolar damage (DAD) pattern: Worst prognosis
   - 72.7% of fatal T-DXd cases had DAD pattern
   - 42.1% of DAD pattern cases fatal
   - Acute interstitial pneumonia (AIP)/ARDS pattern: High mortality (65%)

2. **High Toxicity Grade:**
   - Grade 3: Significant mortality risk
   - Grade 4: 78% 30-day mortality if mechanical ventilation required

3. **Delayed Diagnosis:**
   - Progression from Grade 1 to Grade 2+ associated with worse outcomes
   - Need for permanent ADC discontinuation

4. **Comorbidities:**
   - Pre-existing respiratory disease
   - Renal insufficiency
   - Advanced age (suggested but not definitively proven)

5. **Rapid Onset:**
   - AIP/ARDS pattern has earlier onset (median 17.9 vs 32.7 weeks)
   - Rapid progression over days suggests worse prognosis

---

### 4.3 Long-Term Sequelae and Pulmonary Function

#### **Pulmonary Function Test (PFT) Outcomes**

**Limited Data:**
Long-term PFT outcomes specifically after ADC-induced ILD recovery are not well-characterized in published literature. Most available data comes from other forms of drug-induced ILD or general ILD populations.

**Expected Patterns:**
Based on ILD literature, ADC-induced pneumonitis survivors may demonstrate:
- **Restrictive pattern:** Reduced FVC, reduced TLC
- **Impaired gas exchange:** Reduced DLCO (most common persistent abnormality)
- **Preserved airflow:** Normal FEV1/FVC ratio

**Recovery Timeline:**
- Some patients achieve complete normalization of PFTs
- Others have persistent abnormalities (recovered with sequelae)
- Median time to recovery: 83.5 days (includes clinical and radiographic resolution)

**Prognostic Value:**
In fibrotic ILD, declines in FVC ≥10% and DLCO ≥15% from baseline at 1 year predict increased mortality. Similar thresholds may apply to ADC-ILD survivors, but this requires further study.

---

#### **Residual Radiographic Changes**

Some patients demonstrate persistent radiographic abnormalities despite clinical improvement:
- Residual ground-glass opacities
- Interstitial thickening
- Traction bronchiectasis (suggests fibrosis)
- Honeycombing (rare, indicates advanced fibrosis)

**Clinical Significance:**
Stable residual changes without symptoms may represent chronic scarring rather than active inflammation. However, these patients may be at higher risk for ILD with subsequent ADC exposure.

---

### 4.4 Impact on Cancer Treatment Continuation

#### **Ability to Continue Cancer Therapy**

**Grade 1 Recovery:**
- ADC rechallenge feasible in most cases (67% no recurrence)
- May resume same ADC at same or reduced dose depending on time to resolution
- Cancer treatment continuity best preserved with grade 1 events

**Grade 2 Recovery:**
- Current guidelines: Permanent discontinuation of causative ADC
- Alternative ADCs with different mechanisms may be considered
- Non-ADC cancer therapies (chemotherapy, endocrine, targeted therapy) can typically be safely administered

**Grade 3-4 Recovery:**
- Permanent contraindication to causative ADC
- High risk with any ADC rechallenge
- Alternative ADCs with low ILD risk (e.g., sacituzumab govitecan) may be considered after multidisciplinary discussion
- Non-ADC therapies preferred

---

#### **Cancer Treatment Continuation Rates After ILD**

**Real-World Data:**
In advanced NSCLC patients with pneumonitis from systemic anti-cancer therapy:
- 54% received no further systemic anti-cancer therapy after pneumonitis
- Performance status was poor (ECOG ≥2) in 82% of survivors
- 62% of survivors had worsened performance status after pneumonitis
- Median survival after pneumonitis: 3.5 months

**Interpretation:**
Pneumonitis significantly impacts ability to continue cancer treatment and overall survival. Even if pneumonitis resolves, patients often cannot resume intensive cancer therapy due to:
- Residual pulmonary impairment
- Decline in performance status
- Fear of recurrence
- Cancer progression during treatment interruption

---

#### **Sequential ADC Use**

**Clinical Question:**
After ADC-induced ILD (e.g., from T-DXd), can patients receive alternative ADCs?

**Evidence:**
In a multi-institutional study:
- 4 patients developed ILD on T-DXd, recovered, then received sacituzumab govitecan without recurrent ILD
- 6 patients received sacituzumab govitecan first (no ILD), then T-DXd (developed ILD)

**Interpretation:**
Sequential ADC use is feasible, particularly when switching from high-risk ADC (deruxtecan platform) to low-risk ADC (sacituzumab govitecan). However:
- Requires complete ILD resolution
- Multidisciplinary discussion mandatory
- Intensive monitoring required
- Patient must understand risks
- No guarantees of safety

**Reference:** Abstract PO4-12-06: Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD) in Sequential Antibody Drug Conjugate (ADC) use in patients with Metastatic Breast Cancer. Cancer Res. 2024;84(9_Suppl):PO4-12-06.

## 5. CASE EXAMPLES

### Case Example 1: Grade 1 T-DXd Pneumonitis - Successful Rechallenge

**Presentation:**
A 58-year-old woman with HER2-positive metastatic breast cancer receiving T-DXd 5.4 mg/kg q3 weeks. After cycle 4 (week 12), routine surveillance CT showed new bilateral ground-glass opacities in a peripheral distribution. Patient asymptomatic, no cough, no dyspnea, oxygen saturation 98% on room air.

**Workup:**
- HRCT chest: Bilateral ground-glass opacities consistent with organizing pneumonia pattern
- Infectious workup: Negative (respiratory viral panel, blood cultures, COVID-19)
- Pulmonology consultation: Suspected T-DXd-induced ILD grade 1

**Treatment:**
- T-DXd withheld immediately
- Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day (40 mg daily) initiated
- Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole for PJP prophylaxis

**Outcome:**
- Repeat CT at day 21: Significant improvement in ground-glass opacities
- Repeat CT at day 28: Complete resolution (grade 0)
- Prednisone tapered over 4 weeks
- T-DXd resumed at same dose (5.4 mg/kg) after 35 days total interruption
- Patient completed 12 total cycles without recurrent ILD
- Cancer showed partial response maintained

**Lessons:**
- Proactive monitoring enabled asymptomatic detection
- Early corticosteroid treatment facilitated rapid resolution
- Resolution within 28 days allowed same-dose rechallenge
- Grade 1 rechallenge can be safe with appropriate management

---

### Case Example 2: Grade 3 T-DXd Pneumonitis - Near-Fatal Course

**Presentation:**
A 62-year-old woman with HER2-positive gastric cancer on T-DXd cycle 6. Developed progressive dyspnea and dry cough over 5 days. Presented to emergency department with respiratory distress, oxygen saturation 85% on room air.

**Workup:**
- HRCT chest: Diffuse bilateral ground-glass opacities and consolidations, acute interstitial pneumonia/ARDS pattern
- Infectious workup: Negative
- Bronchoscopy with BAL: No organisms identified, lymphocytic predominance
- Pulmonology and critical care consultation: Grade 3 T-DXd-induced pneumonitis with ARDS pattern

**Treatment:**
- ICU admission
- High-flow nasal cannula (60 L/min, FiO2 60%) to maintain SpO2 >90%
- T-DXd permanently discontinued
- Methylprednisolone 1000 mg IV daily × 3 days
- Transitioned to prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (60 mg daily)
- Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole for PJP prophylaxis
- No improvement by day 5
- Infliximab 5 mg/kg IV added

**Outcome:**
- Gradual improvement over 2 weeks
- Discharged after 18 days on supplemental oxygen (2 L/min)
- Prednisone taper over 8 weeks
- Oxygen discontinued at week 6
- CT at 3 months: Residual ground-glass opacities and interstitial thickening
- PFTs at 3 months: FVC 68% predicted, DLCO 52% predicted (moderate restrictive pattern with impaired gas exchange)
- Cancer treatment switched to chemotherapy (T-DXd contraindicated)
- Cancer progressed 4 months later, patient died 7 months after ILD event

**Lessons:**
- ARDS pattern carries poor prognosis
- Steroid-refractory disease required second-line immunosuppression
- Long recovery time with residual pulmonary impairment
- Permanent contraindication to T-DXd rechallenge
- ILD significantly impacted cancer treatment options and survival

---

### Case Example 3: Successful Sequential ADC Therapy After ILD

**Presentation:**
A 54-year-old woman with HER2-positive/HR-positive metastatic breast cancer. Received T-DXd with excellent cancer response. After cycle 8, developed grade 2 symptomatic pneumonitis (cough, dyspnea on exertion, grade 2).

**Initial Management:**
- T-DXd permanently discontinued
- Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (65 mg daily)
- Complete resolution over 6 weeks (clinical and radiographic)
- Prednisone tapered and discontinued

**Cancer Progression:**
- 3 months after ILD resolution, cancer progressed on endocrine therapy
- Multidisciplinary discussion regarding ADC options
- Patient had HER2+/HR+/TROP2+ breast cancer

**Sequential ADC Decision:**
- Considered sacituzumab govitecan (TROP2-directed, SN-38 payload, very low ILD risk)
- Risks and benefits discussed extensively with patient
- Pulmonology clearance obtained
- Patient agreed to intensive monitoring

**Treatment:**
- Sacituzumab govitecan 10 mg/kg on days 1 and 8 of 21-day cycles
- Baseline CT chest before initiation
- CT surveillance every 6 weeks

**Outcome:**
- Patient completed 6 cycles of sacituzumab govitecan without ILD recurrence
- Cancer showed partial response
- No respiratory symptoms
- Surveillance imaging stable

**Lessons:**
- Sequential ADC use is feasible with appropriate patient selection
- Switching from high-risk ADC (deruxtecan) to low-risk ADC (sacituzumab govitecan) may be safe
- Complete ILD resolution mandatory before considering sequential ADC
- Multidisciplinary discussion essential
- Intensive monitoring required
- Patient understanding and consent critical

## 7. TREATMENT GAPS AND UNMET NEEDS

### 7.1 Evidence Gaps

#### **Lack of Randomized Controlled Trials**

**Current Limitation:**
No randomized controlled trials have been conducted comparing different corticosteroid dosing regimens, taper durations, or steroid vs. no steroid approaches for ADC-induced ILD. All current recommendations are based on:
- Expert consensus
- Extrapolation from immune checkpoint inhibitor pneumonitis
- Clinical trial protocols (not designed to optimize ILD management)
- Observational data

**Unmet Need:**
Prospective trials are needed to establish:
- Optimal corticosteroid dose by grade
- Minimum effective duration of treatment
- Optimal taper schedule
- Role of early intervention for grade 1 disease

**Feasibility Challenges:**
- ILD is a relatively uncommon event (varying by ADC)
- Ethical concerns about withholding corticosteroids from control arm
- Difficulty enrolling patients in acute respiratory distress
- Heterogeneity of ILD presentations

---

#### **Steroid-Refractory Disease Management**

**Current Limitation:**
Evidence for second-line immunosuppressive agents (infliximab, mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide, IVIG) in ADC-induced ILD is extrapolated from:
- Immune checkpoint inhibitor pneumonitis
- Rheumatologic disease-associated ILD
- General drug-induced pneumonitis literature

**Unmet Need:**
- Which second-line agent is most effective?
- What is the optimal timing to add second-line therapy?
- Are there predictive biomarkers for steroid responsiveness?
- What is the role of combination immunosuppression?

**Clinical Uncertainty:**
Current practice varies widely by institution. Some centers use infliximab first-line for steroid-refractory cases (based on ICI-pneumonitis experience), while others prefer mycophenolate or cyclophosphamide.

---

#### **Antifibrotic Therapy Role**

**Current Limitation:**
No data exist on the use of nintedanib or pirfenidone for:
- Prevention of fibrotic progression in ADC-induced ILD
- Treatment of chronic fibrosing changes after ADC pneumonitis
- Adjunctive therapy with corticosteroids

**Unmet Need:**
- Can antifibrotics prevent long-term fibrotic sequelae?
- Should they be initiated early in severe cases?
- What is the role in steroid-refractory progressive fibrosis?

**Potential Research Direction:**
Prospective studies evaluating antifibrotic therapy in patients with:
- Persistent radiographic abnormalities despite corticosteroids
- Progressive fibrosis on serial imaging
- Declining pulmonary function tests after acute resolution

---

#### **Prophylactic Strategies**

**Current Limitation:**
No proven prophylactic interventions exist to prevent ADC-induced ILD. Prophylactic corticosteroids have not been studied.

**Unmet Need:**
- Are there high-risk patients who might benefit from prophylaxis?
- What prophylactic strategies could be effective?
  - Low-dose corticosteroids?
  - Anti-inflammatory agents?
  - Modified ADC dosing schedules?

**Research Directions:**
- Identify biomarkers predicting ILD risk (genetic, protein, imaging)
- Test prophylactic interventions in high-risk populations
- Evaluate modified ADC dosing (lower dose, extended intervals) to reduce ILD incidence while maintaining efficacy

---

### 7.2 Uncertain Treatment Protocols

#### **Optimal Corticosteroid Taper Duration**

**Current Guidance:**
- Minimum 4 weeks taper after resolution
- Some experts recommend 6-8 weeks for severe cases

**Uncertainty:**
- Does taper duration affect relapse risk?
- Can taper be shortened in mild cases?
- What is the optimal taper schedule (e.g., decrease by 10 mg weekly vs. 5 mg every 2 weeks)?

**Clinical Impact:**
Prolonged corticosteroid exposure causes significant toxicity:
- Hyperglycemia (steroid-induced diabetes)
- Bone loss (osteoporosis, fractures)
- Myopathy and weakness
- Infection risk
- Adrenal suppression

Determining the minimum necessary duration could reduce these adverse effects.

---

#### **Rechallenge Decision-Making**

**Current Guidance:**
- Grade 1 with resolution ≤28 days: Rechallenge at same dose generally safe
- Grade 1 with resolution >28 days: Rechallenge at reduced dose
- Grade 2+: Permanent discontinuation recommended

**Uncertainties:**
- Are there Grade 2 patients who could safely be rechallenged?
- What factors predict safe rechallenge? (Pattern on imaging? Corticosteroid responsiveness? Complete vs. partial resolution?)
- Can ADC rechallenge occur after switching to alternative ADC?
- What is the optimal monitoring strategy during rechallenge?

**Real-World Practice Variation:**
Some centers rechallenge grade 2 patients off-protocol, with mixed outcomes. No standardized approach exists, and decision-making is highly individualized.

---

#### **Sequential ADC Safety**

**Current Guidance:**
Essentially none. Major clinical trials exclude patients with prior ADC-induced ILD.

**Uncertainties:**
- Which ADC sequences are safest?
- How long should patients wait after ILD resolution before trying alternative ADC?
- Does ILD with one ADC predict ILD with others?
- Are there predictive factors for safe sequential ADC use?

**Clinical Significance:**
With expanding ADC approvals across tumor types, sequential ADC use will become increasingly common. Evidence-based guidance is critically needed.

**Proposed Research:**
- Registry studies tracking sequential ADC use and ILD outcomes
- Mechanistic studies to understand cross-reactivity
- Development of risk stratification tools

---

#### **PJP Prophylaxis Indications**

**Current Guidance:**
"Consider" PJP prophylaxis for patients on high-dose corticosteroids (≥20 mg prednisone for ≥1 month), particularly pulse steroids.

**Uncertainties:**
- What is the actual risk of PJP in this population?
- Should all patients on corticosteroids for ADC-ILD receive prophylaxis?
- What is the optimal prophylaxis regimen? (Daily vs. three times weekly TMP-SMX? Alternative agents for sulfa-allergic patients?)
- Can prophylaxis be discontinued before completing corticosteroid taper?

**Clinical Impact:**
PJP infection in immunosuppressed cancer patients is potentially fatal. However, TMP-SMX causes adverse effects (nausea, rash, bone marrow suppression, hyperkalemia). Clearer indications would optimize risk-benefit ratio.

---

### 7.3 Biomarkers and Predictive Tools

#### **Early Detection Biomarkers**

**Emerging Data:**
- Lung-derived circulating cell-free DNA (cfDNA) detected using tissue-specific methylation analysis correlates with ADC-induced ILD
- Liquid biopsy may enable earlier detection than imaging or symptoms

**Unmet Needs:**
- Validate cfDNA biomarker in prospective cohorts
- Determine sensitivity and specificity
- Establish cost-effectiveness compared to routine CT surveillance
- Identify other potential blood biomarkers (cytokines, chemokines, surfactant proteins)

**Potential Impact:**
If reliable blood biomarkers exist, they could:
- Enable earlier intervention (pre-radiographic stage)
- Reduce CT scan burden and radiation exposure
- Guide treatment decisions
- Monitor response to corticosteroids

---

#### **Risk Prediction Models**

**Current Limitation:**
No validated risk prediction models exist to identify patients at highest ILD risk before ADC initiation.

**Proposed Risk Factors (Not Validated):**
- Pre-existing respiratory disease (COPD, prior ILD, asthma)
- Smoking history
- Renal insufficiency
- Advanced age
- Asian ethnicity (suggested for T-DXd but inconsistent data)
- Prior thoracic radiation
- Specific ADC type and dose

**Unmet Need:**
Develop and validate multivariable risk prediction models incorporating:
- Clinical factors
- Imaging features (pre-existing lung abnormalities)
- Genetic polymorphisms (pharmacogenomics)
- Baseline pulmonary function tests

**Application:**
Risk models could guide:
- Patient selection for ADC therapy
- Surveillance intensity
- Early intervention strategies
- Alternative treatment choices for high-risk patients

---

#### **Prognosis Prediction at ILD Diagnosis**

**Current Limitation:**
Limited ability to predict which patients will:
- Respond rapidly to corticosteroids
- Develop steroid-refractory disease
- Progress to severe/fatal ILD
- Recover completely vs. have residual sequelae

**Potential Predictive Factors (Require Validation):**
- Radiologic pattern (OP favorable, DAD poor)
- BAL cellular profile
- Biomarkers (KL-6, SP-D, inflammatory cytokines)
- Extent of radiographic involvement
- Time from ADC initiation to ILD onset
- Baseline comorbidities

**Clinical Utility:**
Prognosis prediction at diagnosis could guide:
- Intensity of initial treatment (pulse steroids vs. standard dose)
- Early escalation to second-line agents
- Goals of care discussions
- Clinical trial enrollment

### 7.4 Mechanistic Understanding

#### **Pathophysiology Gaps**

**Current Knowledge:**
- ADCs accumulate in alveolar macrophages after entering lung tissue
- HER2-targeted ADC T-DXd is nonspecifically taken up by alveolar macrophages
- Topoisomerase I inhibitor payload may be particularly toxic to lung tissue
- Immune-mediated mechanisms likely contribute (drug acting as hapten/antigen)

**Critical Unknowns:**
- Why do only some patients develop ILD (10-30% for high-risk ADCs)?
- What determines severity and pattern (OP vs. DAD)?
- Why is topoisomerase I inhibitor payload more toxic to lungs than other payloads?
- Is ILD purely drug-toxicity or immune-mediated, or both?
- What role do host genetic factors play?

**Research Needs:**
- Preclinical models to study mechanisms
- Tissue biobanking from patients with ILD (BAL, lung biopsies)
- Genomic studies to identify susceptibility loci
- Proteomic and transcriptomic analyses of affected lung tissue

**Potential Impact:**
Better mechanistic understanding could lead to:
- Targeted prevention strategies
- Novel treatments beyond corticosteroids
- Biomarkers for risk and prognosis
- Safer ADC design

### 7.5 Quality of Life and Long-Term Outcomes

#### **Patient-Reported Outcomes**

**Current Limitation:**
Few studies have systematically captured patient-reported outcomes (PROs) related to ADC-induced ILD, including:
- Quality of life during and after ILD
- Symptom burden
- Psychological impact (anxiety, fear of recurrence)
- Functional limitations
- Treatment satisfaction

**Unmet Need:**
Prospective collection of PROs using validated instruments to understand:
- Real-world impact on daily life
- Symptom trajectory during recovery
- Long-term quality of life in survivors
- Impact on cancer treatment decision-making

#### **Long-Term Pulmonary Function**

**Current Limitation:**
Limited data on long-term PFT outcomes after ADC-ILD recovery:
- What proportion achieve complete normalization?
- What proportion have persistent restrictive defect or reduced DLCO?
- Do PFT abnormalities progress over time (fibrotic progression)?
- How do PFT outcomes correlate with radiographic patterns?

**Unmet Need:**
Longitudinal cohort studies with serial PFTs at:
- Baseline (before ADC)
- ILD diagnosis
- Post-treatment recovery
- 6 months, 12 months, 24 months

**Clinical Significance:**
Understanding long-term pulmonary function helps:

- Identify patients needing pulmonary rehabilitation
- Determine candidacy for future ADC therapy or lung surgery
- Guide surveillance strategies

Minimal data on cancer survivorship issues after ADC-induced ILD:
- Ability to return to work
- Exercise tolerance and physical activity
- Need for ongoing oxygen or medications
- Psychological sequelae (PTSD from ICU stay, anxiety about respiratory symptoms)

**Unmet Need:**
Survivorship studies addressing:

- Employment and disability
- Quality of life
- Support needs
- Optimal follow-up strategies

### 7.6 Health System and Policy Gaps

#### **Guideline Development**

**Current Limitation:**
While several expert consensus documents exist, formal society guidelines from major organizations (ASCO, ESMO, NCCN) are limited or lack detail on ADC-ILD management.

**Unmet Need:**
Development of comprehensive, evidence-based guidelines by major oncology and pulmonology societies addressing:

- Diagnostic algorithms
- Treatment protocols by grade
- Rechallenge decisions
- Multidisciplinary care models

**Current Limitation:**
Optimal ADC-ILD management requires multidisciplinary teams (oncology, pulmonology, radiology, pathology). Many community cancer centers lack:
- On-site pulmonologists with ILD expertise
- Routine multidisciplinary conferences
- Standardized protocols

**Unmet Need:**
- Dissemination of best practices to community settings
- Telemedicine models for expert pulmonology consultation
- Development of clinical pathways that can be implemented broadly
- Training programs for community providers

---

#### **Regulatory Considerations**

**Current Limitation:**
FDA and EMA regulatory requirements for ILD monitoring and management vary across ADC approvals, creating inconsistency in clinical practice.

**Unmet Need:**
- Standardized ILD monitoring requirements for all ADCs
- Mandatory post-marketing surveillance for ILD outcomes
- Required participation in ILD registries
- Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategies (REMS) for high-risk ADCs

### 7.7 Summary of Priority Research Needs

**Highest Priority:**

1. **Prospective randomized trials** of corticosteroid regimens (dose, duration, taper)

## CONCLUSION

ADC-associated interstitial lung disease represents a significant treatment-related toxicity with substantial morbidity and mortality. Corticosteroids remain the cornerstone of treatment, with dose and duration determined by toxicity grade. Grade 1 (asymptomatic) ILD can often be managed with dose interruption and moderate-dose corticosteroids, allowing potential ADC rechallenge. Grade 2 or higher symptomatic pneumonitis requires permanent ADC discontinuation and prolonged corticosteroid treatment with gradual taper over at least 4 weeks. Severe (grade 3-4) disease necessitates high-dose pulse corticosteroids, hospitalization, and often second-line immunosuppressive agents for steroid-refractory cases.

Key management principles include: (1) early detection through proactive monitoring; (2) prompt treatment initiation to prevent progression; (3) multidisciplinary team coordination; (4) individualized rechallenge decisions based on grade and recovery timeline; and (5) consideration of sequential ADC therapy with lower-risk agents after ILD resolution. Despite recent progress, significant evidence gaps remain regarding optimal corticosteroid protocols, steroid-refractory disease management, biomarkers for risk and prognosis, long-term outcomes, and sequential ADC safety. Future research addressing these unmet needs will further optimize outcomes for patients developing this serious complication.
