## Executive Summary

### Key Findings

This analysis evaluates the predicted safety profile of combining osimertinib, a third-generation EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI), with EGFR-targeting antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) in patients with EGFR-mutant non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Based on individual agent safety profiles and mechanistic considerations, this combination presents significant toxicity concerns:

**Critical Safety Concerns:**
- **Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD):** HIGH risk due to overlapping pulmonary toxicity from both agents. Osimertinib demonstrates 3-4% ILD incidence (all grades, FLAURA trial), while EGFR ADCs show variable incidence (5-26% depending on specific ADC), representing a major dose-limiting toxicity. The combination may produce additive or synergistic pulmonary toxicity.
- **Dermatologic Toxicity:** Predicted cumulative incidence of 70-85% for skin-related adverse events, combining osimertinib's rash (58%) and dry skin (36%) with EGFR ADC skin toxicity (30-50%).
- **Grade ≥3 Adverse Events:** Individual agent rates of 34% (osimertinib, FLAURA) and 20-35% (EGFR ADCs) suggest combination therapy may exceed 45-55% for severe adverse events.

**Evidence Quality:**
- Osimertinib safety: HIGH (Phase III FLAURA trial data, Soria 2018 NEJM)
- EGFR ADC safety: MODERATE (early phase clinical trial data)
- Combination predictions: LOW (no published clinical data; mechanistic extrapolation only)

### Major Concerns

**PRIMARY CONCERN: No published clinical data exists for this specific combination.** All toxicity predictions are based on mechanistic reasoning and individual agent profiles. The true safety profile remains unknown and may differ substantially from predictions.

The most serious concern is ILD risk. Both agents independently cause pulmonary toxicity through EGFR inhibition in lung tissue. The mechanism of overlapping toxicity suggests potential for additive or synergistic effects that could result in:
- Earlier onset of ILD
- Increased severity of pulmonary events
- Higher rates of treatment discontinuation
- Potential for fatal outcomes if not identified early

Additional concerns include cumulative dermatologic toxicity that may significantly impact quality of life and treatment adherence, as well as ocular toxicity from EGFR ADCs (approximately 20% incidence) that requires specialized monitoring.

### Clinical Implications

**This combination should be approached with extreme caution and only within controlled clinical trial settings** until safety data becomes available. If pursued, the following are essential:

1. **Rigorous patient selection:** Exclude patients with baseline pulmonary compromise, prior ILD, or risk factors for pulmonary toxicity
2. **Intensive monitoring:** Weekly clinical assessments and biweekly imaging during initial 8-12 weeks
3. **Proactive dose modifications:** Lower starting doses or sequential rather than concurrent administration should be considered
4. **Immediate escalation pathways:** Protocols for rapid evaluation and management of suspected ILD

The potential benefits of combining these agents must be weighed against substantial safety concerns, with transparent informed consent addressing the absence of clinical safety data.

## Background

### Osimertinib Overview

Osimertinib is a third-generation, irreversible EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitor that selectively targets both EGFR-sensitizing mutations (exon 19 deletions and L858R) and the T790M resistance mutation. Approved for first-line treatment of EGFR-mutant advanced NSCLC, osimertinib demonstrates superior efficacy compared to first-generation EGFR TKIs with improved central nervous system penetration.

The mechanism of action involves covalent binding to the ATP-binding site of mutant EGFR, resulting in sustained kinase inhibition. This selectivity for mutant versus wild-type EGFR theoretically reduces off-target toxicity, though EGFR inhibition in normal tissues (particularly skin, gastrointestinal tract, and lung) still produces characteristic adverse events.

**Clinical Context:**
Osimertinib became standard first-line therapy for EGFR-mutant NSCLC following the FLAURA trial (Soria et al., 2018), which demonstrated significant progression-free survival benefit (18.9 months vs. 10.2 months) compared to first-generation EGFR TKIs. The improved efficacy profile combined with manageable toxicity established osimertinib as the preferred initial EGFR-targeted therapy.

### EGFR-Targeting ADC Overview

EGFR-targeting antibody-drug conjugates represent an emerging therapeutic class combining EGFR-specific monoclonal antibodies with cytotoxic payloads. These agents exploit EGFR overexpression in various solid tumors to deliver potent chemotherapy directly to cancer cells through receptor-mediated internalization.

**Mechanism of Action:**
1. EGFR antibody binds to extracellular domain of EGFR
2. Receptor-ligand complex undergoes endocytosis
3. Intracellular processing releases cytotoxic payload
4. Cytotoxic agent (typically topoisomerase I inhibitors or microtubule inhibitors) causes cell death

**Development Status:**
Multiple EGFR ADCs are in various stages of clinical development, with early phase trials demonstrating activity in EGFR-expressing tumors. However, development has been challenged by significant toxicity, particularly interstitial lung disease, which has emerged as a major dose-limiting toxicity across multiple EGFR-targeting ADC programs.

The payload-dependent toxicity profile varies, but common themes include:
- Pulmonary toxicity (mechanism unclear but consistent across programs)
- Ocular toxicity (potentially related to EGFR expression in ocular tissues)
- Dermatologic toxicity (EGFR-mediated)
- Hematologic toxicity (payload-dependent)

### Rationale for Combination Therapy

The theoretical rationale for combining osimertinib with EGFR ADCs in EGFR-mutant NSCLC includes:

**1. Complementary Mechanisms:**
- Osimertinib: Intracellular kinase inhibition targeting EGFR signaling
- EGFR ADC: Receptor-mediated cytotoxic payload delivery
- Non-overlapping resistance mechanisms may be addressed

**2. Tumor Heterogeneity:**
- Different cellular populations may be preferentially sensitive to each agent
- ADC may target cells with adequate EGFR surface expression regardless of mutation status
- TKI addresses mutation-dependent signaling

**3. Overcoming Resistance:**
- Sequential resistance to EGFR TKIs remains a clinical challenge
- Combination approaches may delay resistance development
- ADC cytotoxic mechanism is independent of kinase domain mutations

**4. Pharmacokinetic Considerations:**
- Minimal predicted drug-drug interactions
- Different elimination pathways
- Potentially compatible dosing schedules

However, these theoretical benefits must be weighed against the significant overlapping toxicity profile, particularly pulmonary toxicity, which may limit the feasibility and safety of combination therapy.

## Individual Safety Profiles

### Osimertinib Safety Profile

**Evidence Base:** HIGH quality evidence from the Phase III FLAURA trial (Soria et al., 2018, New England Journal of Medicine) and supporting clinical trial data.

#### Overall Adverse Event Rates

In the pivotal FLAURA trial, osimertinib demonstrated a manageable safety profile with the following key findings:

- **Grade ≥3 Adverse Events:** 34% of patients (FLAURA trial, n=279 osimertinib arm)
- **Treatment discontinuation due to AEs:** 13% of patients
- **Fatal adverse events:** Rare, with ILD-related deaths 0.4-0.6%
- **Dose interruption:** 25% of patients
- **Dose reduction:** 4% of patients

The rate of severe (Grade ≥3) adverse events was lower than with standard EGFR TKIs (34% vs 45% with comparator), despite longer median duration of exposure, contributing to osimertinib's favorable risk-benefit profile and regulatory approval.

#### Common Adverse Events (All Grades)

**Gastrointestinal Toxicity:**
- **Diarrhea:** 58% incidence (most common adverse event)
  - Generally Grade 1-2 severity
  - Manageable with standard antidiarrheal medications
  - Rarely dose-limiting

**Dermatologic Toxicity:**
- **Rash/Acne:** 58% incidence (Grade ≥3: 1%)
  - Predominantly maculopapular or acneiform
  - Typically Grade 1-2
  - Responsive to topical corticosteroids and supportive care
  - Lower incidence than standard EGFR TKIs (58% vs 78% with comparator)
- **Dry skin:** 36% incidence (Grade ≥3: <1%)
  - Generally mild to moderate
  - Managed with emollients and moisturizers

**Nail changes, pruritus, and paronychia** occur at lower frequencies but contribute to the overall dermatologic toxicity burden.

#### Serious Adverse Events of Special Interest

**Interstitial Lung Disease/Pneumonitis:**
- **All grades:** 3-4% incidence (FDA label: 3.5-4% across studies)
- **Grade ≥3:** <1% incidence
- **Fatal cases:** 0.4-0.6% (FDA prescribing information)

ILD represents the most serious osimertinib-related toxicity. Presentation typically includes:
- New or progressive dyspnea
- Cough
- Fever (in some cases)
- Radiographic findings: Ground-glass opacities, interstitial infiltrates

**Clinical Management:**
- High clinical suspicion required
- Prompt radiographic evaluation
- Immediate osimertinib discontinuation for suspected ILD
- Corticosteroid therapy for confirmed cases
- Permanent discontinuation for confirmed ILD

**Cardiac Toxicity:**
- **QTc prolongation (>480 msec):** 10% incidence
- Generally asymptomatic
- Requires baseline and periodic ECG monitoring
- Dose interruption for Grade ≥3 QTc prolongation
- Risk factors: Baseline QTc prolongation, concomitant QT-prolonging medications, electrolyte abnormalities

**Other Notable Toxicities:**
- Decreased appetite
- Fatigue
- Hematologic effects (generally mild)
- Elevated liver enzymes (uncommon)

#### Risk Factors and Predictors

Specific risk factors for osimertinib-related ILD have not been definitively established, though considerations include:
- Pre-existing pulmonary disease
- Prior thoracic radiation
- Age (potentially)
- Smoking history
- Concurrent lung infections

### EGFR ADC Safety Profile

**Evidence Base:** MODERATE quality evidence from early phase clinical trials (Phase I/II data). Multiple EGFR-targeting ADC programs contribute to this profile, with payload-specific variations.

#### Overall Adverse Event Rates

- **Grade ≥3 Adverse Events:** 20-35% (variable across different EGFR ADC compounds and dose levels)
- **Dose-limiting toxicities:** Primarily ILD and ocular toxicity
- **Treatment discontinuation:** Significant, driven largely by pulmonary toxicity

The range of Grade ≥3 events reflects different ADC constructs (antibody, linker, payload combinations) and dosing strategies explored in early phase trials.

#### Common Adverse Events

**Dermatologic Toxicity:**
- **Estimated incidence:** 30-50%
- Mechanism: EGFR expression in skin
- Manifestations similar to EGFR inhibitor class effects:
  - Acneiform rash
  - Dry skin
  - Pruritus
  - Paronychia

**Ocular Toxicity:**
- **Incidence:** Approximately 20%
- Spectrum of effects:
  - Dry eye
  - Conjunctivitis
  - Keratitis
  - Visual disturbances (less common)
- Mechanism likely related to EGFR expression in ocular tissues
- May require ophthalmologic consultation and topical management

**Gastrointestinal Effects:**
- Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea reported
- Frequency and severity vary by payload type
- Topoisomerase I inhibitor payloads: Higher GI toxicity

**Hematologic Toxicity:**
- Payload-dependent
- Neutropenia, thrombocytopenia reported with certain constructs
- Severity varies by dose level

#### Serious Adverse Events of Special Interest

**Interstitial Lung Disease:**
- **Major dose-limiting toxicity across EGFR ADC programs**
- **Incidence:** Approximately 20% (all grades)
- Significantly higher than conventional EGFR antibodies or chemotherapy
- Clinical presentation:
  - Dyspnea (progressive or acute onset)
  - Cough (dry or productive)
  - Fever (variable)
  - Hypoxia in severe cases
- Radiographic patterns:
  - Ground-glass opacities
  - Diffuse interstitial infiltrates
  - May mimic infectious pneumonitis

**Pathophysiology (Uncertain):**
The mechanism underlying high ILD rates with EGFR ADCs remains incompletely understood. Proposed mechanisms include:
1. On-target toxicity: EGFR expression in pneumocytes and alveolar cells
2. Payload toxicity: Direct cytotoxic effects on lung tissue
3. Immune-mediated reaction: Antibody-dependent inflammation
4. Combination of factors

**Management Challenges:**
- High index of suspicion required
- Difficult to distinguish from infection or disease progression
- Immediate treatment interruption necessary
- Corticosteroid therapy often employed
- Rechallenging after ILD is high-risk

The consistent observation of ILD across different EGFR ADC programs suggests a class effect related to EGFR targeting in the lung, though payload contributions cannot be excluded.

#### Evidence Quality Limitations

Key limitations of EGFR ADC safety data include:
1. **Small sample sizes:** Early phase trial populations
2. **Heterogeneous constructs:** Different antibodies, linkers, payloads
3. **Dose-finding context:** Ongoing optimization of dosing strategies
4. **Selected populations:** Heavily pre-treated patients in early trials
5. **Limited follow-up:** Long-term safety data not yet available

These limitations mean the true safety profile in broader patient populations remains to be fully defined.

## Predicted Combination Toxicities

**CRITICAL LIMITATION: No published clinical data exists for the combination of osimertinib with EGFR-targeting ADCs.** All predictions in this section are based on mechanistic reasoning, overlapping toxicity profiles, and pharmacologic principles. Actual clinical experience may differ substantially.

**Evidence Quality for This Section: LOW**

### Overlapping Toxicity Mechanisms

The combination of osimertinib and EGFR ADCs presents multiple points of overlapping toxicity:

**1. EGFR Inhibition in Normal Tissues:**
Both agents target EGFR, which is expressed in normal epithelial tissues including:
- Skin and appendages
- Gastrointestinal mucosa
- Lung (pneumocytes, alveolar cells)
- Ocular tissues

**2. Dermatologic Convergence:**
- Osimertinib: EGFR kinase inhibition in skin
- EGFR ADC: EGFR-mediated uptake and potential bystander effects
- Additive mechanism predicts higher cumulative skin toxicity

**3. Pulmonary Toxicity Convergence:**
- Osimertinib: EGFR inhibition in lung tissue (3% ILD rate)
- EGFR ADC: EGFR-targeted delivery to lung + payload toxicity (20% ILD rate)
- Potential for synergistic pulmonary injury

### Interstitial Lung Disease Risk Assessment

**CRITICAL SAFETY CONCERN**

#### Mechanistic Basis for Concern

The convergence of pulmonary toxicity mechanisms creates substantial ILD risk:

**Osimertinib Contribution:**
- Inhibits EGFR signaling in type II pneumocytes
- Disrupts alveolar homeostasis
- 3% ILD incidence as monotherapy

**EGFR ADC Contribution:**
- EGFR-mediated targeting to lung tissues
- Delivery of cytotoxic payload to pneumocytes
- Potential bystander killing of adjacent cells
- 20% ILD incidence as monotherapy

**Combined Effect Predictions:**

1. **Independent Events Model (Lower Bound):**
   - Combined ILD risk: ~22% [calculated as 1 - (1-0.03)(1-0.20) = 0.224]
   - Assumes independent toxicity mechanisms with no interaction
   - Likely underestimates true risk given shared mechanism (EGFR inhibition)

2. **Synergistic Model (Upper Bound):**
   - Combined ILD risk: 35-50%
   - Assumes mechanistic interaction amplifies risk
   - Based on overlapping EGFR inhibition in lung
   - Supported by common toxicity pathway

3. **Most Likely Scenario:**
   - ILD incidence: 25-35%
   - Grade ≥3 pulmonary events: 10-15%
   - Treatment discontinuation due to ILD: 15-20%

#### Clinical Characteristics of Predicted ILD

**Onset:**
- Likely earlier than either monotherapy
- High-risk period: First 2-3 months of combination therapy
- Possibility of acute presentation

**Severity:**
- Higher proportion of Grade ≥3 events
- Greater risk of respiratory failure
- Increased mortality risk

**Management Challenges:**
- Difficult attribution (which agent?)
- Both agents may require discontinuation
- Rechallenge highly problematic
- Permanent treatment discontinuation likely

#### Risk Stratification

**High-Risk Patients (SHOULD EXCLUDE FROM COMBINATION):**
- Prior ILD or pneumonitis from any cause
- Baseline interstitial lung abnormalities
- Significant pre-existing pulmonary disease (COPD, pulmonary fibrosis)
- Prior thoracic radiation
- Active or recent pulmonary infection
- Concurrent use of other pulmonary-toxic medications

**Moderate-Risk Patients:**
- Age >70 years
- Smoking history >20 pack-years
- Multiple pulmonary comorbidities

**Lower-Risk Patients:**
- Young age (<60 years)
- Never smokers
- Normal baseline pulmonary function
- No prior thoracic disease or treatment

#### Predicted Incidence

**Combined Dermatologic Toxicity: 70-85% (all grades)**

**Rationale:**

- Osimertinib dry skin: 36% (FLAURA trial)

## Clinical Recommendations

Given the absence of clinical safety data and significant predicted toxicity concerns, these recommendations prioritize patient safety while acknowledging potential therapeutic rationale.

### Patient Selection Criteria

#### Mandatory Inclusion Criteria

1. **Confirmed EGFR-mutant NSCLC** with documented EGFR expression suitable for ADC targeting
2. **ECOG performance status 0-1**
3. **Adequate organ function:**
   - Normal baseline pulmonary function (FEV1 >70% predicted, DLCO >60%)
   - Adequate hepatic function (AST/ALT <2.5× ULN)
   - Adequate renal function (CrCl >50 mL/min)
   - Adequate bone marrow function
4. **Normal baseline chest CT** (no pre-existing interstitial changes)
5. **QTc <450 msec** (male) or <470 msec (female)

#### Absolute Exclusion Criteria

1. **Prior ILD or pneumonitis** from any cause
2. **Baseline interstitial lung disease** on imaging
3. **Significant pulmonary comorbidities:**
   - Severe COPD (FEV1 <50% predicted)
   - Pulmonary fibrosis
   - Active pulmonary infection
4. **Prior thoracic radiation** within 6 months
5. **Concurrent pulmonary-toxic medications** (e.g., bleomycin, amiodarone)
6. **Significant cardiac disease** or baseline QTc >470 msec
7. **ECOG performance status ≥2**

#### Relative Exclusion Criteria (Careful Consideration Required)

- Age >75 years
- Smoking >20 pack-years
- Prior treatment with other EGFR TKIs complicated by Grade ≥3 toxicity
- Multiple comorbidities

### Monitoring Strategy

#### Baseline Assessments

**Before Initiating Combination Therapy:**
1. **Pulmonary:**
   - High-resolution chest CT
   - Pulmonary function tests (spirometry, DLCO)
   - Baseline oxygen saturation
2. **Cardiac:**
   - ECG with QTc measurement
   - Consider echocardiogram if cardiac history
3. **Ophthalmologic:**
   - Baseline eye examination (if resources available)
4. **Laboratory:**
   - Complete blood count
   - Comprehensive metabolic panel
   - Liver function tests
5. **Patient Education:**
   - Detailed discussion of ILD symptoms
   - Written instructions for concerning symptoms
   - 24/7 contact information

#### Intensive Monitoring Phase (Weeks 1-12)

**Clinical Assessments:**
- **Weekly visits** for first 4 weeks
- **Biweekly visits** for weeks 5-12
- Each visit: Pulmonary symptom assessment, oxygen saturation, vital signs

**Imaging:**
- **Chest CT: Week 2, Week 4, Week 8, Week 12**
- More frequent if any concerning symptoms
- Low threshold for additional imaging

**Laboratory:**
- **Weekly:** CBC, electrolytes
- **Biweekly:** Liver function tests
- **ECG:** Week 2, Week 4, Week 8, then monthly

**Patient-Reported:**
- Daily symptom diary
- Immediate reporting of:
  - New or worsening dyspnea
  - New cough
  - Fever
  - Chest discomfort

#### Maintenance Monitoring Phase (After Week 12)

**Clinical Assessments:**
- **Monthly clinic visits**
- Continued symptom surveillance

**Imaging:**
- **Chest CT every 6-8 weeks**
- Continue vigilance for delayed ILD

**Laboratory:**
- **Monthly:** CBC, CMP, LFTs
- **ECG:** Every 2-3 months

### Dose Modification Guidelines

#### Starting Doses (Consider Dose Reduction)

**Approach 1: Standard Dosing with Intensive Monitoring**
- Osimertinib: 80 mg once daily
- EGFR ADC: Per standard dosing for specific compound
- Only if patient meets all inclusion criteria

**Approach 2: Conservative Dosing (RECOMMENDED)**
- Osimertinib: Start at 80 mg once daily
- EGFR ADC: Start at 75% of standard dose
- Escalate ADC dose if tolerated after 2 cycles

**Approach 3: Sequential Administration (SAFEST)**
- Establish osimertinib tolerance for 4 weeks
- Add EGFR ADC after confirming baseline stability
- Allows attribution of toxicities

#### Dose Interruption Triggers

**Immediate Interruption of BOTH Agents:**
- Any suspected ILD (new pulmonary symptoms + imaging changes)
- Grade ≥3 pulmonary toxicity
- Grade 4 any toxicity
- QTc >500 msec or >60 msec increase from baseline

**Interrupt ADC Only:**
- Grade 3 ocular toxicity
- Grade 3 dermatologic toxicity unresponsive to management
- Neutropenic fever

**Interrupt Osimertinib Only:**
- Isolated QTc prolongation 480-500 msec
- Grade 3 diarrhea unresponsive to antidiarrheals

#### Dose Reduction Guidelines

**For Dermatologic Toxicity (Grade 3):**
1. Interrupt both agents until improvement to Grade ≤1
2. Resume osimertinib at 40 mg once daily
3. Resume ADC at 75% dose
4. If recurrent: Consider discontinuing ADC, continuing osimertinib

**For Diarrhea (Grade 3):**
1. Interrupt both agents, aggressive supportive care
2. Resume with dose reductions as above
3. Prophylactic antidiarrheals upon resumption

**For Confirmed ILD:**
- **PERMANENT DISCONTINUATION of both agents**
- No rechallenge
- Systemic corticosteroids (prednisone 1 mg/kg or equivalent)

## Limitations and Uncertainties

### Critical Data Gaps

**1. No Clinical Experience with This Combination**

The single most important limitation of this analysis is the complete absence of published clinical data combining osimertinib with EGFR-targeting ADCs. All toxicity predictions are theoretical, based on:
- Individual agent safety profiles
- Mechanistic reasoning about overlapping toxicity pathways
- Extrapolation from other drug combinations

**The actual safety profile may differ substantially from predictions** in either direction:
- Toxicity could be less than predicted if mechanisms do not actually overlap
- Toxicity could be more severe if synergistic effects occur
- Novel, unexpected toxicities may emerge that were not predicted

**2. Heterogeneity of EGFR ADC Class**

"EGFR ADCs" represent a class of agents with significant variability:
- Different monoclonal antibodies (binding epitopes, affinity)
- Different linker technologies (cleavable vs. non-cleavable)
- Different cytotoxic payloads (topoisomerase inhibitors, tubulin inhibitors, etc.)
- Different drug-to-antibody ratios

The safety profile summarized for "EGFR ADCs" aggregates data across multiple constructs. The specific ADC used in combination with osimertinib would substantially influence the toxicity profile. This analysis cannot account for construct-specific differences.

**3. Limited Understanding of ILD Pathophysiology**

While ILD is recognized as a significant toxicity for both osimertinib and EGFR ADCs, the underlying mechanisms remain incompletely understood:
- Contribution of on-target vs. off-target effects unclear
- Role of immune-mediated injury uncertain
- Genetic or biomarker predictors of ILD not established
- Dose-dependency relationships not fully characterized

Without mechanistic clarity, predicting combination effects remains highly speculative.

**4. Pharmacokinetic and Pharmacodynamic Interactions**

The analysis assumes minimal PK interactions based on different elimination pathways, but:
- No actual PK studies of this combination exist
- PD interactions at the cellular level are possible
- Impact on EGFR target engagement unknown
- Payload disposition in the presence of TKI unknown

**5. Optimal Dosing and Scheduling Uncertainty**

Even if this combination proceeds to clinical testing, optimal administration remains unknown:
- Concurrent vs. sequential administration
- Dose modifications or reductions from standard monotherapy doses
- Intermittent vs. continuous dosing strategies
- Lead-in periods with one agent

These variables could substantially influence the safety profile.

## Conclusions

The combination of osimertinib with EGFR-targeting antibody-drug conjugates presents substantial predicted toxicity concerns, most critically a high risk of interstitial lung disease. With individual agent ILD rates of 3-4% (osimertinib, FLAURA trial) and 5-26% (EGFR ADCs, depending on construct), overlapping pulmonary toxicity mechanisms suggest combination ILD rates may reach 25-35%, with increased severity and potential for fatal outcomes.

Additional predicted toxicities include cumulative dermatologic effects (70-85% incidence based on osimertinib's 58% rash + 36% dry skin), gastrointestinal toxicity (60-70% diarrhea), and ocular effects (20-25%). The overall predicted Grade ≥3 adverse event rate may exceed 45-55% (baseline osimertinib 34% from FLAURA), substantially higher than either agent alone.

**Critical Limitation:** No published clinical data exists for this combination. All predictions are mechanistic extrapolations that may not accurately reflect actual clinical outcomes.

**Recommendation:** This combination should only be pursued within rigorously designed clinical trials with comprehensive safety monitoring, restrictive patient selection criteria, and predetermined stopping rules for unacceptable toxicity. The intensity of monitoring required and the magnitude of predicted risks necessitate careful patient counseling and transparent informed consent that acknowledges the absence of safety data.

Future research to elucidate ILD mechanisms, develop predictive biomarkers, and optimize dosing strategies is essential before this combination could be considered for broader clinical use.
