# Antibody-Drug Conjugate-Associated Interstitial Lung Disease: Mechanisms, Clinical Management, and Prevention Strategies in 2025

**A Comprehensive Review and Clinical Practice Guide**

---

## ABSTRACT

**Background:** Antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs) represent a transformative class of targeted cancer therapeutics, combining the specificity of monoclonal antibodies with potent cytotoxic payloads. However, interstitial lung disease (ILD) has emerged as a dose-limiting and potentially fatal toxicity, with incidence rates ranging from less than 1% to over 28% depending on ADC structure and patient factors. Understanding the mechanistic basis, risk stratification, clinical management, and prevention strategies for ADC-associated ILD has become critical as these agents gain widespread clinical adoption.

**Methods:** This comprehensive review synthesizes evidence from clinical trials, real-world studies, pharmacovigilance databases, and mechanistic research published between 2020-2025, with emphasis on 2023-2025 data. We systematically reviewed ADC-induced ILD epidemiology, molecular mechanisms, clinical presentation, diagnostic approaches, treatment protocols, and prevention strategies across all approved and investigational ADCs.

**Key Findings:** ADC-associated ILD demonstrates marked heterogeneity in incidence (0.4%-28.1%), severity, and outcomes across different agents. Topoisomerase I inhibitor payloads (deruxtecan platform) carry the highest risk, with trastuzumab deruxtecan demonstrating 10-15% ILD incidence and 2.2% mortality rate despite improved monitoring protocols. Multiple interconnected mechanisms contribute to lung toxicity, including non-specific macrophage uptake via Fcγ receptors, payload-mediated pneumocyte injury, bystander cytotoxicity, and immune-mediated inflammation. Clinical presentation ranges from asymptomatic radiographic findings (grade 1) to acute respiratory distress syndrome (grade 4), with diffuse alveolar damage pattern conferring 42% mortality risk. Corticosteroids remain the cornerstone of treatment, with dose and duration stratified by toxicity grade. Grade 1 ILD allows ADC rechallenge after resolution, while grade 2 or higher mandates permanent discontinuation under current guidelines. Mortality rates for severe ILD remain unacceptably high (30-day mortality: 78% for mechanically ventilated patients; 6-month mortality: 96%), underscoring the critical importance of early detection and prevention.

**Conclusions:** ADC-associated ILD represents a complex, multifactorial toxicity requiring multidisciplinary management, proactive monitoring, and individualized treatment decisions. Emerging biomarkers (circulating cell-free DNA, serum KL-6) may enable earlier detection, while mechanistic insights suggest potential prevention strategies. Significant knowledge gaps remain regarding optimal treatment protocols, predictive risk factors, long-term outcomes, and safety of sequential ADC use. Future research must address these unmet needs to optimize the therapeutic index of this promising drug class.

**Keywords:** Antibody-drug conjugates, interstitial lung disease, pneumonitis, trastuzumab deruxtecan, drug-induced lung injury, cancer immunotherapy toxicity

## 1. INTRODUCTION

### 1.1 The ADC Revolution in Oncology

Antibody-drug conjugates represent one of the most significant advances in targeted cancer therapy over the past decade. By linking highly potent cytotoxic payloads to tumor-specific monoclonal antibodies through chemical linkers, ADCs achieve preferential delivery of chemotherapy to malignant cells while theoretically sparing normal tissues.1,2 This elegant pharmacologic strategy has translated into remarkable clinical efficacy across multiple solid and hematologic malignancies, including breast cancer, lung cancer, bladder cancer, ovarian cancer, and acute leukemias.3,4

The ADC landscape has expanded dramatically since 2019, with 15 agents currently approved by regulatory authorities worldwide and over 100 ADCs in clinical development.5,6 Notable recent approvals include trastuzumab deruxtecan (Enhertu, 2019), sacituzumab govitecan (Trodelvy, 2020), tisotumab vedotin (Tivdak, 2021), mirvetuximab soravtansine (Elahere, 2022), and datopotamab deruxtecan (Dato-DXd, 2024).7-10 These agents have demonstrated unprecedented response rates in heavily pretreated populations, with some achieving objective response rates exceeding 60% in chemotherapy-refractory disease.11,12

However, the therapeutic promise of ADCs is tempered by unique toxicity profiles that differ from both conventional chemotherapy and monoclonal antibody therapy. Among these adverse events, interstitial lung disease has emerged as a particularly concerning complication due to its potential severity, unpredictable onset, and significant mortality risk.13,14

### 1.2 ILD as Dose-Limiting Toxicity

Interstitial lung disease, also termed drug-induced pneumonitis, represents a spectrum of inflammatory and fibrotic conditions affecting the pulmonary parenchyma and alveolar structures. In the context of ADC therapy, ILD encompasses a range of pathologic patterns including organizing pneumonia, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, diffuse alveolar damage, and nonspecific interstitial pneumonia.15,16

The recognition of ADC-associated ILD as a clinically significant toxicity emerged prominently with early trastuzumab deruxtecan trials. Initial registrational studies (DESTINY-Breast01, DESTINY-Gastric01) reported ILD incidence of 13.6% (any grade) with 2.7% grade 5 (fatal) events, leading to intensive safety monitoring requirements and treatment modifications.17,18 Subsequent real-world experience and enhanced surveillance protocols have identified ILD rates ranging from 10-28% depending on monitoring intensity, though grade 5 rates have decreased to 0.8% in recent trials with proactive management.19,20

Critically, ADC-associated ILD differs from pneumonitis associated with other cancer therapies in several key aspects. Unlike immune checkpoint inhibitor-induced pneumonitis, which typically responds robustly to immunosuppression, ADC-ILD demonstrates variable corticosteroid responsiveness with substantial steroid-refractory disease.21 Unlike conventional chemotherapy-induced lung injury (e.g., bleomycin, methotrexate), ADC-ILD can occur after any treatment cycle without clear cumulative dose relationships.22 The unpredictability of onset, severity, and outcomes makes ADC-ILD particularly challenging for clinicians managing these increasingly prevalent agents.

### 1.3 Scope and Objectives

This comprehensive review provides an evidence-based synthesis of current knowledge regarding ADC-associated interstitial lung disease as of 2025. Our objectives are to:

1. **Characterize the epidemiology** of ILD across approved and investigational ADCs, identifying incidence rates, time-to-onset patterns, risk factors, and mortality outcomes
2. **Elucidate molecular and cellular mechanisms** underlying ADC lung toxicity, integrating preclinical and clinical data
3. **Define clinical presentation and diagnostic approaches** for early detection and accurate characterization
4. **Establish evidence-based treatment protocols** stratified by toxicity grade and ADC type
5. **Review prevention strategies** including baseline screening, surveillance monitoring, and risk mitigation
6. **Provide ADC-specific management guidance** for the most commonly used agents
7. **Identify knowledge gaps** and research priorities to advance the field

This review synthesizes data from pivotal clinical trials, real-world cohort studies, pharmacovigilance analyses, mechanistic research, expert consensus statements, and regulatory documents published through January 2025. We emphasize actionable clinical guidance while acknowledging uncertainties and limitations in current evidence.

### 1.4 Clinical Significance

Understanding and effectively managing ADC-associated ILD has profound implications for oncology practice. As ADCs become incorporated into earlier lines of therapy and potentially curative settings, the risk-benefit calculus shifts. An adverse event that might be acceptable in the context of metastatic, chemotherapy-refractory disease becomes less tolerable in patients with better prognosis and alternative treatment options.23,24

Moreover, the expanding ADC pipeline raises questions about sequential ADC use and cross-toxicity. Can patients who develop ILD with one ADC safely receive alternative ADCs? What monitoring is required? These questions lack definitive answers but have immediate clinical relevance as oncologists navigate complex treatment algorithms with multiple ADC options.25

Finally, ADC-associated ILD serves as a case study in pharmacovigilance, risk management, and adaptive clinical practice. The evolution from initial high mortality rates to improved outcomes through enhanced monitoring exemplifies how systematic attention to toxicity can improve therapeutic index without compromising efficacy.26 The lessons learned from ADC-ILD management inform broader approaches to novel cancer therapy complications.

## 2. EPIDEMIOLOGY AND RISK FACTORS

### 2.1 Incidence Rates Across ADC Portfolio

The incidence of drug-related interstitial lung disease varies dramatically across different ADCs, ranging from less than 1% for some agents to over 28% for others depending on tumor type, patient population, and monitoring intensity.27,28 This heterogeneity reflects fundamental differences in ADC structure (antibody, linker, payload, drug-antibody ratio), target antigen expression patterns, patient factors, and surveillance protocols.

#### 2.1.1 High-Risk ADCs (ILD Incidence >10%)

**Trastuzumab Deruxtecan (T-DXd, Enhertu):** Across nine monotherapy studies, adjudicated drug-related ILD occurred in 15.4% of patients (any grade), with 3.7% grade 3-4 events and 2.2% grade 5 (fatal) events.29 In breast cancer specifically (DESTINY-Breast series), ILD rates range from 10.8% to 15.6% depending on prior treatment lines and monitoring protocols.30,31 Real-world studies with intensive monitoring have detected ILD rates as high as 28.1%, suggesting that many cases in clinical trials may have been subclinical or undetected.32 Importantly, proactive monitoring and early intervention in recent trials have reduced grade 5 rates to 0.8%, demonstrating that enhanced vigilance improves outcomes.33

**Datopotamab Deruxtecan (Dato-DXd):** In the TROPION-Lung01 trial for previously treated non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), adjudicated drug-related ILD occurred in 8.8% of patients overall, with grade ≥3 events in 3.4%.34 Notably, grade 5 ILD occurred in 2.4% of patients, a concerning signal that prompted regulatory discussions regarding risk mitigation strategies. The ILD profile of dato-DXd appears qualitatively similar to trastuzumab deruxtecan, consistent with the shared deruxtecan payload platform.35

**Mirvetuximab Soravtansine (Elahere):** Pneumonitis occurred in 10% of patients in the SORAYA and FORWARD I trials, with 1% grade 3 and 0.1% grade 4 events.36 The toxicity led to permanent discontinuation in 3% of patients. The maytansinoid payload (DM4) appears to confer moderate ILD risk compared to other payload classes.

#### 2.1.2 Moderate-Risk ADCs (ILD Incidence 3-10%)

**Enfortumab Vedotin (Padcev):** As monotherapy for urothelial carcinoma, pneumonitis occurred in 3% of patients (0.8% grade 3-4).37 However, when combined with pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor) in the EV-302 trial, pneumonitis incidence increased to 10% overall with 4% grade 3-4 events, suggesting synergistic pulmonary toxicity with immune checkpoint inhibitors.38 Median time to onset was 4 months (range 0.3-26 months), demonstrating that ILD can occur at any point during therapy.

**Tisotumab Vedotin (Tivdak):** Among 720 patients treated with this tissue factor-directed ADC for cervical cancer, pneumonitis occurred in 3% overall (0.8% grade 3-4), with one fatal outcome (0.14%).39 The relatively low ILD rate compared to other MMAE-containing ADCs may reflect differences in target antigen distribution or patient population.

#### 2.1.3 Lower-Risk ADCs (ILD Incidence <3%)

**Sacituzumab Govitecan (Trodelvy):** This TROP2-directed ADC with SN-38 payload demonstrates remarkably low pulmonary toxicity. In the phase 3 ASCENT trial (metastatic triple-negative breast cancer), ILD occurred in less than 1% of patients, with only one case of reversible grade 3 pneumonitis in a patient with multiple confounding factors.40 No sacituzumab govitecan-related deaths from ILD have been reported. This favorable safety profile makes sacituzumab govitecan an attractive option for sequential ADC therapy after ILD from other agents.

**Brentuximab Vedotin (Adcetris):** This CD30-directed ADC with MMAE payload, approved since 2011 for Hodgkin lymphoma and anaplastic large cell lymphoma, has very low ILD incidence (<1%) in clinical trials.41 Rare cases of fatal pulmonary toxicity have been reported in post-marketing surveillance, but the overall pulmonary safety profile remains favorable.

**Gemtuzumab Ozogamicin (Mylotarg):** While this CD33-directed ADC for acute myeloid leukemia shows low overall ILD incidence, pharmacovigilance data indicate extremely high mortality when ILD does occur (57.14% case fatality rate).42 This highlights that low incidence does not equate to low clinical significance.

**Ado-Trastuzumab Emtansine (Kadcyla):** This first-generation HER2-directed ADC demonstrates minimal pulmonary toxicity, with ILD reported in less than 1% of patients in pivotal trials.43 The contrast with trastuzumab deruxtecan, which targets the same antigen but uses different linker and payload, underscores the critical importance of ADC structural components in determining toxicity profiles.

### 2.2 Payload Class Correlations

Accumulating evidence suggests that cytotoxic payload type is a primary determinant of ILD risk, with topoisomerase I inhibitors demonstrating the highest pulmonary toxicity.44,45

#### 2.2.1 Topoisomerase I Inhibitors (Deruxtecan Platform)

The deruxtecan payload, a derivative of exatecan (topoisomerase I inhibitor), appears intrinsically associated with elevated ILD risk. Both trastuzumab deruxtecan (HER2-targeting) and datopotamab deruxtecan (TROP2-targeting) demonstrate ILD rates of 8-28%, despite targeting different antigens expressed in different tumor types and normal tissues.46,47 This consistent pattern across different antibodies strongly implicates the payload as the key toxicity driver.

Mechanistic explanations for topoisomerase I inhibitor lung toxicity include:
- High membrane permeability enabling bystander effect on neighboring cells
- Particular vulnerability of pneumocytes to topoisomerase I inhibition
- Generation of reactive oxygen species during drug metabolism
- Enhanced macrophage uptake and activation

Preclinical studies in cynomolgus monkeys demonstrated that T-DXd accumulated in lung tissue at levels comparable to tumor tissue, with preferential uptake by alveolar macrophages despite absence of HER2 expression on these cells.48 This off-target distribution provides mechanistic insight into clinical lung toxicity.

#### 2.2.2 Maytansinoids (DM1, DM4)

Maytansinoids, which inhibit microtubule assembly, demonstrate intermediate ILD risk. Mirvetuximab soravtansine (DM4 payload) shows 10% pneumonitis incidence, while ado-trastuzumab emtansine (DM1 payload) demonstrates less than 1% ILD.49,50 The divergent toxicity profiles despite similar payload class suggest that antibody selection, linker chemistry, and drug-antibody ratio modulate pulmonary toxicity even within payload classes.

#### 2.2.3 Auristatins (MMAE, MMAF)

Monomethyl auristatin E (MMAE) and MMAF payloads, which inhibit tubulin polymerization, generally confer lower ILD risk. Enfortumab vedotin (MMAE) shows 3% monotherapy ILD incidence, tisotumab vedotin (MMAE) shows 3%, and brentuximab vedotin (MMAE) demonstrates less than 1%.51-53 However, the combination of enfortumab vedotin with immune checkpoint inhibitors increases ILD to 10%, suggesting that auristatin-related pneumonitis may be potentiated by immune modulation.

#### 2.2.4 Calicheamicin and Other DNA-Damaging Agents

Gemtuzumab ozogamicin, carrying a calicheamicin payload (DNA-cleaving agent), shows low ILD incidence but very high mortality when ILD occurs (57% case fatality rate).54 Inotuzumab ozogamicin (also calicheamicin-containing) demonstrates similar pattern with 50% mortality in pharmacovigilance reports.55 The severity of ILD with calicheamicin-conjugates may reflect the potency of DNA damage induced in pulmonary cells.

#### 2.2.5 SN-38 (Topoisomerase I Inhibitor, Non-Deruxtecan)

Sacituzumab govitecan utilizes SN-38, the active metabolite of irinotecan, as its cytotoxic payload. Despite SN-38 being a topoisomerase I inhibitor like deruxtecan, sacituzumab govitecan demonstrates remarkably low ILD incidence (<1%).56 Critical structural differences include:
- Cleavable linker (CL2A) versus tetrapeptide-based cleavable linker
- Lower drug-antibody ratio (DAR ~7.6) compared to T-DXd (DAR ~8)
- Different membrane permeability and bystander effect potential
- Rapid systemic clearance of released payload

This comparison demonstrates that payload class alone does not determine toxicity—linker chemistry and pharmacokinetic properties substantially modulate safety profiles.

### 2.3 Linker Chemistry and Cleavability

ADC linkers serve as critical determinants of payload release kinetics, biodistribution, and toxicity. Linkers are broadly classified as cleavable (sensitive to intracellular conditions) or non-cleavable (requiring complete antibody degradation).57,58

#### 2.3.1 Cleavable Linkers

**Tetrapeptide-based cleavable linkers** (used in T-DXd and dato-DXd) are designed to be cleaved by lysosomal cathepsins after antibody internalization. However, these linkers may undergo premature cleavage in circulation or extracellular space, particularly in inflammatory microenvironments such as tumor stroma or inflamed lung tissue.59 Prematurely released payload can enter neighboring cells through passive diffusion (the "bystander effect"), potentially affecting pneumocytes and other lung cells adjacent to macrophages containing internalized ADC.

**Acid-labile hydrazone linkers** (used in gemtuzumab ozogamicin, inotuzumab ozogamicin) are cleaved in acidic endosomal/lysosomal compartments. While this mechanism should restrict payload release to intracellular compartments, some premature release in acidic tumor microenvironment or phagolysosomes can occur.60

**Disulfide linkers** (used in brentuximab vedotin) are cleaved by glutathione and other reducing agents enriched intracellularly. This mechanism generally provides good selectivity for tumor cells while limiting systemic exposure.61

#### 2.3.2 Non-Cleavable Linkers

Non-cleavable linkers, such as the thioether linker in ado-trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1), require complete proteolytic degradation of the antibody to release active payload.62 This mechanism may reduce bystander effect and off-target toxicity, potentially explaining the very low ILD rate with T-DM1 compared to T-DXd despite identical antibody component.

The contrast between T-DM1 (non-cleavable, <1% ILD) and T-DXd (cleavable, 10-28% ILD) provides compelling evidence that linker chemistry profoundly influences pulmonary toxicity, even when the targeting antibody is identical.

#### 2.3.3 Site-Specific Conjugation

Traditional ADCs use stochastic conjugation to lysine or cysteine residues, resulting in heterogeneous drug-antibody ratios and conjugation sites. Emerging site-specific conjugation technologies (e.g., engineered cysteine, unnatural amino acids, enzymatic conjugation) enable precise control over DAR and conjugation location.63 Preliminary evidence suggests that site-specific ADCs may have improved therapeutic index with reduced toxicity, though ILD-specific data remain limited.

### 2.4 Drug-Antibody Ratio (DAR) Effects

The number of payload molecules conjugated per antibody (DAR) influences both efficacy and toxicity. Higher DAR increases cytotoxic potency but may also increase off-target toxicity through enhanced bystander effect, altered pharmacokinetics, or immune activation.64,65

Trastuzumab deruxtecan has DAR of approximately 8, among the highest of approved ADCs.66 This high payload loading contributes to remarkable anti-tumor efficacy but may also enhance pulmonary toxicity through:
- Increased payload concentration in lung tissue after ADC uptake
- Enhanced bystander killing of adjacent pneumocytes
- Greater potential for premature linker cleavage releasing multiple payload molecules

Sacituzumab govitecan has similar DAR (~7.6) but dramatically lower ILD incidence, indicating that DAR alone does not determine pulmonary toxicity—payload properties and linker chemistry remain paramount.67

### 2.5 Patient Risk Factors

Identifying patients at elevated ILD risk could enable personalized monitoring strategies and inform treatment selection. However, robust, prospectively validated risk factors remain elusive.68,69

#### 2.5.1 Pre-Existing Respiratory Disease

**Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, prior ILD:** Patients with baseline pulmonary pathology might be expected to have reduced pulmonary reserve and increased susceptibility to ADC-induced lung injury. However, clinical trial data are inconsistent. Some analyses show higher ILD rates in patients with smoking history or baseline lung abnormalities, while others find no significant association.70,71 Most pivotal trials excluded patients with active ILD or severe respiratory disease, limiting generalizability.

**Prior thoracic radiation:** Radiation-induced lung injury creates areas of fibrosis and inflammation that could be susceptible to additional insults. Case series describe severe ILD in patients receiving ADC therapy shortly after thoracic radiation, but systematic data are lacking.72

**COVID-19 history:** Given recent pandemic exposure, concerns have emerged about post-COVID lung injury increasing ADC-ILD risk. No published data specifically address this question, but pulmonologists managing ADC patients report anecdotal associations.

**UNCERTAINTY NOTE:** Despite biologic plausibility, definitive evidence linking pre-existing respiratory disease to ADC-ILD risk is limited. Clinical trials systematically excluded high-risk respiratory patients, creating evidence gaps for real-world populations.

#### 2.5.2 Smoking History

Smoking status has shown inconsistent associations with ADC-ILD risk. Some analyses suggest current or former smokers have elevated risk, potentially due to chronic airway inflammation, macrophage activation, or subclinical parenchymal damage.73 However, other studies find no significant difference in ILD incidence between smokers and non-smokers.74

Current smoking status is often an exclusion criterion in ADC trials, further limiting evidence. No clear recommendations exist regarding smoking cessation before ADC initiation, though smoking cessation remains generally advisable for cancer patients.

#### 2.5.3 Age

Early analyses of T-DXd suggested higher ILD risk in elderly patients, but this association has not been consistently validated in larger cohorts.75 Age-related changes in lung function, comorbidities, and drug metabolism could theoretically modulate ILD risk, but chronologic age alone appears insufficient for risk stratification.

#### 2.5.4 Ethnicity and Geographic Factors

Initial T-DXd trials in Japan reported higher ILD incidence compared to Western trials, prompting speculation about ethnic susceptibility factors.76 However, subsequent analyses suggest that differences reflect enhanced monitoring and detection in Japanese studies rather than true biologic predisposition. When comparable surveillance protocols are applied, ILD rates are similar across ethnic groups.77

#### 2.5.5 Renal Insufficiency

Impaired renal function could alter ADC pharmacokinetics, potentially increasing systemic exposure and toxicity. Pharmacokinetic analyses show minimal impact of mild-moderate renal impairment on T-DXd exposure, and ILD rates do not clearly correlate with baseline renal function.78 However, severe renal insufficiency (CrCl <30 mL/min) remains understudied as these patients are typically excluded from trials.

#### 2.5.6 Concomitant Medications

**Immune checkpoint inhibitors:** Combination of ADCs with anti-PD-1/PD-L1 antibodies appears to increase pneumonitis risk. Enfortumab vedotin combined with pembrolizumab shows 10% ILD rate compared to 3% with enfortumab vedotin monotherapy.79 Mechanistically, checkpoint inhibitors may augment immune-mediated components of ADC pneumonitis.

**Corticosteroids:** Paradoxically, patients receiving baseline corticosteroids for other indications do not appear protected from ADC-ILD, suggesting that inflammatory pathways targeted by steroids may not be initiating events in ILD pathogenesis.

**Other pneumotoxic drugs:** Concurrent or recent exposure to other pneumotoxic medications (bleomycin, amiodarone, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin) could theoretically create additive lung injury, but systematic data are absent.

#### 2.5.7 Tumor-Related Factors

**Lung metastases:** Presence of pulmonary metastases complicates ILD diagnosis (distinguishing drug toxicity from tumor progression, infection, or lymphangitic carcinomatosis) but does not clearly increase ILD incidence.80

**Prior lines of therapy:** Heavily pretreated patients might have cumulative organ damage from prior treatments, but most analyses find no correlation between number of prior regimens and ILD risk.81

### 2.6 Time-to-Onset Patterns

ADC-associated ILD can occur at any time during treatment, from the first cycle to months after therapy completion. Understanding temporal patterns informs surveillance strategies.82,83

#### 2.6.1 Median Time to Onset

**Trastuzumab deruxtecan:** Median time to onset of first ILD event is approximately 4.1-5.4 months (range: 0.7-30.6 months) across clinical trials.84 However, real-world series report earlier median onset around 2-3 months, potentially reflecting enhanced surveillance detecting earlier cases.85

**Datopotamab deruxtecan:** Similar time-to-onset profile as T-DXd, with median around 4-5 months.86

**Enfortumab vedotin:** Median time to onset of 4 months (range 0.3-26 months), with no clear early versus late onset pattern.87

**Mirvetuximab soravtansine:** ILD events occurred throughout treatment, with no defined high-risk period.88

#### 2.6.2 Early Versus Late Onset

Most ILD cases occur within the first 6-12 months of therapy, but late-onset cases (>12 months) comprise approximately 10-20% of events.89 Late-onset ILD presents clinical challenges as patients and providers may have lower vigilance after prolonged uneventful treatment.

Importantly, ILD can occur after treatment discontinuation, with cases reported up to 2-3 months following last ADC dose.90 This necessitates continued surveillance for several months after stopping therapy.

#### 2.6.3 Radiologic Pattern and Time-to-Onset

Diffuse alveolar damage (DAD) and acute interstitial pneumonia (AIP) patterns tend to have earlier onset (median 17.9 weeks) and more acute presentation compared to organizing pneumonia (OP) or hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP) patterns (median 32.7 weeks).91 Early-onset, rapidly progressive ILD correlates with higher mortality risk.

### 2.7 Mortality Outcomes

ADC-associated ILD carries substantial mortality risk, particularly when severe respiratory compromise develops.92,93

#### 2.7.1 Overall Case Fatality Rates

Across all ADCs and all ILD grades, overall mortality from drug-related pneumonitis is approximately 26%.94 However, this aggregate figure masks dramatic heterogeneity:

**By ADC:**
- Trastuzumab deruxtecan: 2.2% grade 5 rate (decreased from 2.7% in early trials to 0.8% in recent trials with proactive monitoring)95,96
- Datopotamab deruxtecan: 2.4% grade 5 rate in TROPION-Lung0197
- Gemtuzumab ozogamicin: 57.14% case fatality rate (though very low incidence)98
- Inotuzumab ozogamicin: 50.00% case fatality rate99
- Sacituzumab govitecan: No reported deaths from ILD in clinical trials100

**By ILD Grade:**
- Grade 1-2: <1% mortality with appropriate management
- Grade 3: 10-15% mortality
- Grade 4: 30-day mortality 78%, 6-month mortality 96% if mechanical ventilation required101

**By Radiologic Pattern:**
- Organizing pneumonia (OP): <5% mortality, generally steroid-responsive
- Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (HP): ~5% mortality
- Diffuse alveolar damage (DAD): 42.1% mortality, often steroid-refractory102
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS): 65% mortality103

#### 2.7.2 Temporal Trends in Mortality

Importantly, mortality rates have decreased substantially as awareness, monitoring protocols, and management strategies have improved. Early T-DXd trials reported 2.7% grade 5 ILD, while recent trials with mandatory baseline CT, frequent surveillance imaging, and rapid intervention protocols show 0.8% grade 5 rates—a 70% reduction.104 This demonstrates that systematic attention to toxicity management can dramatically improve outcomes without compromising efficacy.

#### 2.7.3 Factors Associated with Fatal Outcomes

Multivariate analyses identify several factors associated with ILD mortality:
- Diffuse alveolar damage pattern on radiology (strongest predictor)
- Delayed diagnosis (progression from grade 1 to grade 3-4)
- ARDS presentation requiring mechanical ventilation
- Steroid-refractory disease
- Pre-existing respiratory comorbidities
- Delayed treatment initiation
- Concurrent infections (bacterial, viral, fungal)

Conversely, factors associated with favorable outcomes include:
- Early detection at grade 1 (asymptomatic) stage
- Organizing pneumonia pattern
- Rapid steroid responsiveness
- Proactive monitoring enabling early intervention
- Multidisciplinary management team

### 2.8 Summary of Epidemiologic Findings

ADC-associated ILD demonstrates marked heterogeneity in incidence (0.4%-28.1%), with topoisomerase I inhibitor payloads (deruxtecan platform) conferring highest risk. Linker chemistry and drug-antibody ratio modulate toxicity independent of payload class. Patient-level risk factors remain poorly defined, with no validated risk prediction model currently available. Time-to-onset ranges from first cycle to months after treatment cessation, necessitating sustained vigilance. Overall mortality is 26%, but ranges from <1% for grade 1-2 disease with appropriate management to 78% for grade 4 disease requiring mechanical ventilation. Temporal trends show decreasing mortality as proactive monitoring and management protocols improve, underscoring the value of systematic toxicity management.

**EVIDENCE LIMITATIONS:** Most epidemiologic data derive from clinical trial populations with exclusion of high-risk respiratory patients. Real-world incidence, risk factors, and outcomes in unselected populations require further characterization. Risk prediction models require prospective validation.

## 3. MOLECULAR AND CELLULAR MECHANISMS

Understanding the molecular basis of ADC-induced lung toxicity is critical for developing prevention strategies, identifying biomarkers, and designing safer ADCs. Current evidence indicates that ILD results from multiple interconnected mechanisms involving non-specific ADC uptake, payload-mediated cell death, bystander effects, and immune-mediated inflammation.105,106

### 3.1 Fcγ Receptor-Mediated Macrophage Uptake

A fundamental mechanism underlying ADC pulmonary toxicity is non-specific uptake by alveolar macrophages via Fcγ receptors, independent of target antigen expression.107,108

#### 3.1.1 Macrophage Biology and Fcγ Receptors

Alveolar macrophages serve as sentinel immune cells in lung tissue, constituting the first line of defense against inhaled pathogens and particulates. These cells express high levels of Fcγ receptors (FcγRI, FcγRII, FcγRIII), which bind the Fc portion of IgG antibodies to enable phagocytosis of antibody-opsonized particles.109

When ADCs enter lung tissue (via capillary permeability, especially in inflamed or malignant tissue), alveolar macrophages recognize and internalize them through Fcγ receptor-mediated endocytosis, even when macrophages do not express the ADC's target antigen.110 This represents off-target drug uptake and accumulation.

#### 3.1.2 Preclinical Evidence

Kumagai et al. (2020) demonstrated that trastuzumab deruxtecan accumulated in lungs of cynomolgus monkeys at concentrations comparable to tumor tissue, despite absence of HER2 expression on normal lung cells.111 Immunohistochemistry revealed T-DXd preferentially localized within alveolar macrophages. Similar findings have been replicated with datopotamab deruxtecan and other ADCs with Fc-intact antibodies.112

Mechanistic studies show:
- ADC uptake by macrophages is Fcγ receptor-dependent (blocked by Fc receptor antagonists)
- After internalization, ADCs undergo lysosomal degradation, releasing cytotoxic payload
- Released payload kills the macrophage or escapes to neighboring cells (bystander effect)
- Dead and dying macrophages release inflammatory mediators (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, reactive oxygen species)
- Inflammatory signals recruit additional immune cells (neutrophils, lymphocytes)
- Chronic inflammation progresses to fibrosis if not resolved

#### 3.1.3 Target Antigen-Independent Toxicity

The Fcγ receptor mechanism explains why ADCs targeting different antigens (HER2, TROP2, FRα) produce similar pulmonary toxicity patterns. The antibody component serves as a vehicle for delivering payload to lungs independent of target antigen distribution. This contrasts with typical ADC mechanism of action, where antigen expression on tumor cells determines drug delivery.

Supporting this interpretation:
- Trastuzumab deruxtecan (anti-HER2) and datopotamab deruxtecan (anti-TROP2) show similar ILD incidence and patterns despite different targets
- ILD occurs in tumor types with low HER2 expression (gastric cancer) at similar rates as high HER2 tumors (breast cancer)
- Lung tissue from ILD patients shows macrophage accumulation and activation regardless of ADC target antigen

#### 3.1.4 Implications for ADC Design

The Fcγ receptor mechanism suggests potential mitigation strategies:
- Engineering Fcγ receptor-null antibodies (Fc mutations reducing FcγR binding)
- Using antibody fragments lacking Fc region (Fab, scFv conjugates)
- Developing non-antibody targeting vehicles (peptides, aptamers, small molecules)

However, Fc effector functions (antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity, complement-dependent cytotoxicity) contribute to ADC anti-tumor activity, so eliminating Fc function may reduce efficacy. This creates a challenging optimization problem balancing toxicity and efficacy.113

### 3.2 Linker Chemistry and Payload Release

The ADC linker determines where and when cytotoxic payload is released, profoundly influencing toxicity profiles.114,115

#### 3.2.1 Premature Linker Cleavage

Cleavable linkers designed for intracellular payload release can undergo premature cleavage in circulation, extracellular space, or inflammatory microenvironments. Tetrapeptide-based linkers in T-DXd and dato-DXd are substrates for cathepsin enzymes, which are upregulated in inflamed tissue and tumor stroma.116 Premature cleavage releases free payload that can:
- Enter nearby cells via passive diffusion (bystander effect)
- Accumulate in lung tissue due to high capillary surface area
- Directly injure pneumocytes, endothelial cells, and other lung cells

Pharmacokinetic studies show that a small fraction of deruxtecan payload (~5-10%) circulates in free form after linker cleavage, which could contribute to off-target toxicity.117

#### 3.2.2 Bystander Effect

The bystander effect—the ability of released payload to kill neighboring cells—is intended to enhance anti-tumor efficacy by targeting tumor cells with low antigen expression. However, this mechanism also enables toxicity to non-target tissues.118

Deruxtecan payloads are highly membrane-permeable, enabling efficient bystander killing. After macrophage uptake and degradation, released deruxtecan can diffuse into adjacent pneumocytes (type I and type II alveolar epithelial cells), causing cytotoxicity even though pneumocytes lack target antigen expression.

In contrast, ado-trastuzumab emtansine (T-DM1) has a non-cleavable linker requiring complete antibody degradation, limiting bystander effect. This may explain T-DM1's minimal pulmonary toxicity compared to T-DXd.119

#### 3.2.3 Payload-Specific Mechanisms

Different cytotoxic payloads have distinct mechanisms of action and cellular vulnerabilities:

**Topoisomerase I Inhibitors (Deruxtecan):**
- Trap topoisomerase I-DNA complexes, causing DNA double-strand breaks during replication
- Pneumocytes may be particularly vulnerable due to active proliferation for repair/renewal
- Topoisomerase I inhibition generates reactive oxygen species, contributing to oxidative injury
- SN-1, the active catabolite of irinotecan/SN-38, crosses cell membranes readily, enhancing bystander effect

**Maytansinoids (DM1, DM4):**
- Inhibit microtubule assembly, blocking mitosis
- Relatively less membrane-permeable than deruxtecan, reducing bystander effect
- May accumulate in lysosomes, limiting cytoplasmic exposure

**Auristatins (MMAE, MMAF):**
- Inhibit tubulin polymerization
- MMAE is more membrane-permeable than MMAF, potentially explaining higher toxicity
- Generally less pulmonary toxic than topoisomerase I inhibitors, though mechanism unclear

**Calicheamicin:**
- Induces DNA double-strand breaks through free radical generation
- Extremely potent, requiring very low concentrations for cell killing
- High mortality rate when ILD occurs may reflect potency of DNA damage

### 3.3 Pneumocyte Injury and Apoptosis

Alveolar epithelial cells (pneumocytes) are critical for gas exchange and alveolar homeostasis. Type I pneumocytes cover 95% of alveolar surface area, while type II pneumocytes produce surfactant and serve as progenitor cells for repair.120

#### 3.3.1 Direct Cytotoxic Injury

ADC payloads released from macrophages or delivered via premature linker cleavage can directly kill pneumocytes through:
- DNA damage (topoisomerase I inhibitors, calicheamicin)
- Mitotic catastrophe (maytansinoids, auristatins)
- Oxidative stress and mitochondrial dysfunction
- Activation of apoptotic pathways (caspase-3, caspase-9)

Histopathologic studies of ADC-induced ILD show pneumocyte apoptosis, sloughing of alveolar epithelium, and denuded alveolar basement membranes—direct evidence of epithelial cell death.121

#### 3.3.2 Impaired Surfactant Production

Type II pneumocytes produce pulmonary surfactant, which reduces surface tension and prevents alveolar collapse. Injury to type II cells impairs surfactant production, contributing to:
- Alveolar collapse (atelectasis)
- Impaired gas exchange
- Increased work of breathing
- Predisposition to acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)

Biomarker studies show elevated serum surfactant protein-D (SP-D) levels in patients developing ADC-induced ILD, suggesting type II pneumocyte injury.122

#### 3.3.3 Impaired Epithelial Regeneration

Type II pneumocytes serve as progenitor cells for alveolar repair, differentiating into type I pneumocytes after injury. ADC-mediated type II cell death impairs regenerative capacity, potentially explaining progression from acute injury to chronic fibrosis when epithelial repair fails.123

### 3.4 Macrophage Polarization and Inflammatory Signaling

Beyond direct cytotoxicity, ADCs induce complex inflammatory responses involving macrophage activation and cytokine signaling.124,125

#### 3.4.1 Macrophage Death and Inflammatory Mediator Release

When alveolar macrophages internalize ADC and undergo apoptosis/necrosis from payload toxicity, they release damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) and inflammatory cytokines:
- IL-1β (pyroptosis marker)
- IL-6 (acute phase inflammatory cytokine)
- TNF-α (pro-inflammatory cytokine)
- Reactive oxygen species
- Protease enzymes

These mediators recruit neutrophils, lymphocytes, and monocytes to lung tissue, amplifying inflammation.126

#### 3.4.2 M1/M2 Macrophage Polarization

Macrophages can adopt pro-inflammatory (M1) or pro-fibrotic (M2) phenotypes depending on microenvironmental signals. In ADC-induced ILD:
- Early phase: M1 polarization predominates (inflammatory response)
- Late phase: M2 polarization may drive fibrotic progression

Transcriptomic studies of ADC-exposed lung tissue show upregulation of M2 markers (CD163, CD206) and pro-fibrotic mediators (TGF-β, PDGF) in chronic cases, suggesting a pathologic shift from inflammation to fibrosis.127

#### 3.4.3 SPP1 (Secreted Phosphoprotein 1) Pathway

Recent mechanistic studies identify SPP1 (osteopontin) as a key mediator of ADC-induced lung injury. SPP1 is upregulated in macrophages and pneumocytes following ADC exposure and promotes:
- Macrophage recruitment and activation
- Epithelial-mesenchymal transition (EMT) in pneumocytes
- Fibroblast activation and collagen deposition
- Resistance to apoptosis in fibroblasts

Targeting the SPP1 pathway in preclinical models attenuates ADC-induced lung injury, suggesting potential therapeutic opportunity.128

### 3.5 Immune-Mediated Mechanisms

In addition to direct cytotoxicity, immune-mediated mechanisms likely contribute to ADC-induced ILD, particularly cases resembling hypersensitivity pneumonitis.129,130

#### 3.5.1 Type IV Hypersensitivity Reaction

Some ADC-ILD cases show histologic features consistent with hypersensitivity pneumonitis: lymphocytic infiltration, granuloma formation, and bronchiolocentric distribution. This suggests a type IV (delayed-type) hypersensitivity reaction where:
- ADC or its metabolites act as haptens
- Haptenized proteins are processed and presented by antigen-presenting cells
- CD4+ T cells recognize hapten-protein complexes
- T cell-mediated inflammation ensues

Supporting this mechanism:
- BAL from some ADC-ILD patients shows lymphocytic alveolitis (>50% lymphocytes)
- CD4+ T cell predominance in BAL and lung biopsies
- Recurrence of ILD upon rechallenge (immunologic memory)
- Response to corticosteroids (immunosuppression)

#### 3.5.2 Antibody-Mediated Responses

Rare cases of ADC-ILD show features suggestive of antibody-mediated lung injury:
- Vasculitis or capillaritis on biopsy
- Complement deposition in alveolar walls
- Rapid improvement with plasma exchange (case reports)

These findings suggest that in some patients, anti-ADC antibodies or immune complexes contribute to lung injury.131

#### 3.5.3 Synergy with Checkpoint Inhibitors

The increased ILD incidence when ADCs are combined with immune checkpoint inhibitors (e.g., enfortumab vedotin + pembrolizumab) suggests that T cell activation potentiates ADC lung toxicity. Potential mechanisms include:
- Enhanced antigen presentation of ADC-modified proteins
- Increased cytotoxic T cell activity against injured pneumocytes
- Amplified cytokine storm
- Reduced regulatory T cell suppression

### 3.6 Fibrotic Progression

A subset of ADC-ILD cases progress to pulmonary fibrosis despite corticosteroid treatment, resulting in chronic restrictive lung disease.132,133

#### 3.6.1 Epithelial-Mesenchymal Transition (EMT)

Injured alveolar epithelial cells can undergo EMT, losing epithelial characteristics and acquiring mesenchymal/fibroblast-like properties. EMT contributes to:
- Loss of epithelial barrier function
- Myofibroblast accumulation
- Excessive extracellular matrix deposition
- Irreversible fibrotic remodeling

TGF-β, a master regulator of EMT and fibrosis, is upregulated in ADC-injured lung tissue.134

#### 3.6.2 Myofibroblast Activation

Fibroblasts and myofibroblasts are activated by pro-fibrotic mediators (TGF-β, PDGF, CTGF) released by injured epithelium, inflammatory cells, and macrophages. Activated myofibroblasts produce excessive collagen and other extracellular matrix proteins, creating irreversible fibrotic scarring.135

#### 3.6.3 Failed Resolution of Inflammation

Normally, acute lung injury resolves through:
- Clearance of inflammatory cells
- Epithelial regeneration
- Matrix remodeling

In some ADC-ILD cases, resolution fails due to:
- Persistent stimulus (continued ADC exposure if not discontinued)
- Impaired epithelial regeneration (type II pneumocyte death)
- Dysregulated macrophage function (persistent M2 polarization)
- Genetic or environmental factors promoting fibrosis

The factors determining which patients progress to fibrosis versus complete resolution remain incompletely understood.

### 3.7 Biomarkers and Molecular Signatures

Identifying molecular biomarkers of ADC-induced ILD could enable early detection, risk stratification, and mechanistic insights.136,137

#### 3.7.1 Serum Biomarkers

**KL-6 (Krebs von den Lungen-6):** A mucin-like glycoprotein expressed by type II pneumocytes and bronchiolar epithelium. Elevated serum KL-6 correlates with various forms of ILD, including drug-induced pneumonitis. In ADC-ILD, KL-6 elevations have been reported but lack sufficient sensitivity/specificity for clinical use. Further validation is needed.138

**Surfactant Protein-D (SP-D):** Produced by type II pneumocytes, SP-D levels increase with alveolar epithelial injury. Elevated SP-D has been observed in ADC-ILD but, like KL-6, requires prospective validation.139

**Circulating Cell-Free DNA (cfDNA) with Tissue-Specific Methylation:** Nakamura et al. (2024) demonstrated that detection of lung-derived cfDNA using tissue-specific methylation patterns correlated with T-DXd-induced ILD.140 This liquid biopsy approach may enable earlier detection than imaging or symptoms, potentially allowing pre-symptomatic intervention. This represents one of the most promising emerging biomarkers but requires multicenter validation.

**Cytokines and Chemokines:** Elevations in IL-6, IL-8, MCP-1, and other inflammatory mediators have been reported in some ADC-ILD patients but lack specificity (elevated in many inflammatory conditions).141

**EVIDENCE LIMITATION:** No serum biomarker is currently validated for clinical use in ADC-ILD detection or monitoring. Published studies are small, retrospective, and lack standardized cutoffs or validation cohorts.

#### 3.7.2 BAL Cellular Analysis

Bronchoalveolar lavage (BAL) provides direct sampling of alveolar compartment:
- Lymphocytic alveolitis (>50% lymphocytes) suggests hypersensitivity pattern
- Neutrophilic alveolitis suggests acute inflammatory injury
- Eosinophilic alveolitis suggests drug hypersensitivity (though rare in ADC-ILD)
- CD4/CD8 ratio can help distinguish patterns (elevated in HP, decreased in sarcoidosis)

BAL is useful for excluding infection and characterizing ILD pattern but not routinely performed for ADC-ILD diagnosis due to invasiveness and variable utility.142

#### 3.7.3 Genetic Susceptibility Markers

No pharmacogenomic markers predicting ADC-ILD susceptibility have been validated. Candidate genes include:
- MUC5B promoter variant rs35705950 (associated with idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis risk)
- Surfactant protein gene polymorphisms
- Cytochrome P450 variants affecting payload metabolism
- HLA alleles associated with drug hypersensitivity

Genome-wide association studies (GWAS) in ADC-treated cohorts with detailed ILD phenotyping could identify susceptibility loci but have not been performed to date.143

### 3.8 Preclinical Models

Understanding ADC-ILD mechanisms requires appropriate animal models, though recapitulating human lung toxicity in animals remains challenging.144,145

#### 3.8.1 Non-Human Primate Models

Cynomolgus monkeys represent the most translational preclinical model, as demonstrated by Kumagai et al.'s T-DXd studies showing lung accumulation, macrophage uptake, and pneumonitis.146 However, primate studies are expensive, low-throughput, and face ethical constraints limiting widespread use.

#### 3.8.2 Rodent Models

Mouse and rat models have been explored but show limited ADC-induced lung toxicity, possibly due to:
- Species differences in pulmonary anatomy and immune function
- Different Fcγ receptor expression patterns
- Faster drug metabolism
- Shorter lifespan limiting chronic toxicity detection

Humanized mouse models with human FcγR expression or human lung tissue xenografts may better recapitulate human toxicity.147

#### 3.8.3 In Vitro Models

Human lung organoids, air-liquid interface cultures of primary human pneumocytes, and co-culture systems (macrophages + pneumocytes) enable mechanistic studies of ADC toxicity in human cells. These models have demonstrated:
- Direct pneumocyte cytotoxicity from ADC payloads
- Macrophage-mediated bystander killing
- Inflammatory cytokine release
- Surfactant production impairment

While valuable for mechanism dissection, in vitro models lack the complexity of intact lung tissue and systemic factors.148

### 3.9 Integrative Mechanistic Model

Synthesizing current evidence, we propose an integrative model of ADC-induced ILD:

**Phase 1 (Initiation, Days 1-7):**
1. ADC administered IV, distributes systemically
2. ADC extravasates into lung tissue via capillary permeability
3. Alveolar macrophages internalize ADC via Fcγ receptors (target-independent)
4. Intracellular linker cleavage releases cytotoxic payload
5. Macrophages undergo apoptosis/necrosis, releasing inflammatory mediators
6. Payload diffuses to adjacent pneumocytes (bystander effect)
7. Pneumocyte injury and apoptosis ensue

**Phase 2 (Inflammation, Days 7-28):**
8. DAMPs and cytokines recruit neutrophils, monocytes, and lymphocytes
9. Inflammatory cascade amplifies (IL-1β, IL-6, TNF-α, reactive oxygen species)
10. Additional pneumocyte death from cytokine toxicity and oxidative stress
11. Impaired surfactant production and alveolar dysfunction
12. Clinical symptoms emerge (cough, dyspnea) and radiographic changes appear (ground-glass opacities)
13. If ADC continued: ongoing cycles of macrophage uptake and injury

**Phase 3a (Resolution, Weeks 4-12):**
14. ADC discontinued and corticosteroids initiated
15. Anti-inflammatory effects suppress cytokine production
16. Inflammatory cell apoptosis and clearance
17. Type II pneumocyte proliferation and differentiation
18. Epithelial repair and surfactant restoration
19. Radiographic improvement and symptom resolution

**Phase 3b (Fibrotic Progression, Weeks 4+):**
14. Failed epithelial regeneration (extensive type II cell death)
15. Persistent M2 macrophage activation
16. TGF-β-driven EMT and myofibroblast activation
17. Excessive collagen deposition and extracellular matrix remodeling
18. Irreversible fibrotic scarring
19. Chronic restrictive physiology and impaired gas exchange

**Modifying Factors:**
- ADC structure (payload, linker, DAR) determines toxicity magnitude
- Genetic factors influence susceptibility and inflammatory responses
- Baseline lung health affects reserve and repair capacity
- Timing of diagnosis and intervention determines outcomes
- Concomitant medications (checkpoint inhibitors) amplify toxicity

**UNCERTAINTY ACKNOWLEDGMENT:** This model integrates multiple lines of evidence but remains incomplete. Key unknowns include: Why do only 10-30% of exposed patients develop ILD? What genetic or environmental factors determine individual susceptibility? What is the relative contribution of direct cytotoxicity versus immune-mediated mechanisms? Why do some patients progress to fibrosis while others resolve completely? Answering these questions requires additional mechanistic research, biomarker studies, and genetic analyses.

### 3.10 Implications for Prevention and Treatment

Mechanistic understanding informs rational approaches to prevention and treatment:

**Prevention Strategies:**
- Engineering Fcγ-null ADCs to reduce macrophage uptake
- Optimizing linker chemistry to minimize premature cleavage
- Developing less membrane-permeable payloads to limit bystander effect
- Identifying genetic susceptibility markers for personalized risk assessment

**Treatment Strategies:**
- Early ADC discontinuation to stop ongoing injury
- Corticosteroids to suppress inflammatory cascade
- Antifibrotic agents (nintedanib, pirfenidone) to prevent/slow fibrotic progression
- Targeted therapies against SPP1 or TGF-β pathways (investigational)
- Surfactant replacement therapy (theoretical)

**Biomarker Development:**
- Validating serum markers (KL-6, SP-D, cfDNA) for early detection
- Pharmacogenomic testing to identify high-risk patients
- Imaging biomarkers to predict progression risk

## 4. CLINICAL PRESENTATION AND DIAGNOSIS

Early recognition of ADC-associated ILD is critical for optimal outcomes. Clinical presentation ranges from asymptomatic radiographic findings to life-threatening respiratory failure, necessitating high vigilance and systematic diagnostic approaches.149,150

### 4.1 Symptom Spectrum

#### 4.1.1 Asymptomatic (Grade 1)

Approximately 30-40% of ADC-ILD cases are detected as asymptomatic radiographic findings during routine surveillance imaging, highlighting the value of proactive monitoring.151 Patients have:
- No respiratory symptoms
- Normal oxygen saturation (≥95% on room air)
- Normal exercise tolerance
- Detection only through chest CT abnormalities

Asymptomatic detection enables early intervention before progression to symptomatic disease, with best outcomes and highest rechallenge success rates.

#### 4.1.2 Mild Symptoms (Early Grade 2)

Early symptomatic disease presents with nonspecific respiratory complaints:
- **Dry cough:** Persistent, non-productive, not responsive to cough suppressants
- **Mild dyspnea on exertion:** Shortness of breath with activities previously well-tolerated (climbing stairs, brisk walking)
- **Fatigue:** Potentially related to hypoxemia or inflammatory cytokines
- **Normal resting oxygen saturation:** May have exertional desaturation

These symptoms are easily dismissed as deconditioning, anxiety, or minor respiratory infection, emphasizing the need for low threshold for evaluation in ADC-treated patients.

#### 4.1.3 Moderate Symptoms (Grade 2)

Established symptomatic ILD presents with:
- **Progressive dyspnea:** Shortness of breath at rest or with minimal activity
- **Persistent cough:** May be productive in cases with secondary infection
- **Oxygen requirement:** SpO2 <90% on room air, requiring supplemental oxygen
- **Tachypnea:** Respiratory rate >20 breaths/minute
- **Limitation of activities of daily living:** Instrumental ADLs affected (shopping, cooking, household chores)

Physical examination may reveal:
- Tachypnea and increased work of breathing
- Bibasilar inspiratory crackles (velcro-like rales)
- Oxygen desaturation with ambulation
- Otherwise unremarkable cardiac and pulmonary examination (no wheezing, no peripheral edema)

#### 4.1.4 Severe Symptoms (Grade 3-4)

Life-threatening ILD presents with acute respiratory distress:
- **Severe dyspnea at rest:** Unable to speak in full sentences
- **Hypoxemic respiratory failure:** SpO2 <90% despite supplemental oxygen
- **Tachypnea:** Respiratory rate >30 breaths/minute
- **Accessory muscle use:** Intercostal retractions, nasal flaring
- **Altered mental status:** Confusion or lethargy from hypoxemia
- **Hemodynamic instability:** Tachycardia, hypotension in severe cases

This presentation constitutes a medical emergency requiring ICU admission and aggressive intervention.

### 4.2 Radiologic Patterns

High-resolution computed tomography (HRCT) is essential for diagnosing and characterizing ADC-induced ILD. Multiple radiologic patterns occur, each with distinct prognostic implications.152,153

#### 4.2.1 Organizing Pneumonia (OP) Pattern

**Radiographic Features:**
- Bilateral patchy consolidations and ground-glass opacities
- Peripheral and peribronchovascular distribution
- "Reversed halo sign" or "atoll sign" in some cases (ring of consolidation surrounding ground-glass opacity)
- Subpleural sparing may occur

**Clinical Correlation:**
- Most common pattern in ADC-ILD (approximately 40-50% of cases)
- Generally good prognosis
- Responsive to corticosteroids
- Low mortality (<5%)
- Median time to onset: 4-6 months

**Histopathologic Correlate:**
Granulation tissue plugs within alveolar ducts and alveoli (Masson bodies), preservation of lung architecture, variable interstitial inflammation.

#### 4.2.2 Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (HP) Pattern

**Radiographic Features:**
- Bilateral ground-glass opacities
- Poorly defined centrilobular nodules
- Mosaic attenuation (patchy areas of decreased attenuation due to air trapping)
- Relatively symmetric distribution

**Clinical Correlation:**
- Approximately 20-30% of ADC-ILD cases
- Suggests type IV hypersensitivity mechanism
- Generally steroid-responsive
- Moderate prognosis
- May recur upon rechallenge (immunologic memory)

**Histopathologic Correlate:**
Lymphocytic bronchiolitis, peribronchiolar metaplasia, interstitial inflammation, poorly formed non-necrotizing granulomas in some cases.

#### 4.2.3 Diffuse Alveolar Damage (DAD) Pattern

**Radiographic Features:**
- Diffuse bilateral ground-glass opacities and consolidation
- Symmetric distribution
- Rapid progression over days
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS) pattern

**Clinical Correlation:**
- Approximately 15-20% of ADC-ILD cases
- **Worst prognosis: 42% mortality rate**
- Often steroid-refractory
- Rapid onset (median 17.9 weeks, earlier than other patterns)
- Requires ICU management
- 72.7% of fatal T-DXd cases had DAD pattern

**Histopathologic Correlate:**
Acute phase: hyaline membrane formation, pneumocyte necrosis, interstitial edema, capillary congestion. Organizing phase: type II pneumocyte hyperplasia, fibroblast proliferation, early fibrosis.

#### 4.2.4 Nonspecific Interstitial Pneumonia (NSIP) Pattern

**Radiographic Features:**
- Bilateral ground-glass opacities with reticular interstitial thickening
- Lower lobe predominance
- Subpleural sparing
- Relatively symmetric

**Clinical Correlation:**
- Less common in acute ADC-ILD (~10-15% of cases)
- May represent subacute or chronic presentation
- Variable steroid responsiveness
- Risk of progression to fibrosis

**Histopathologic Correlate:**
Temporally uniform interstitial inflammation and/or fibrosis, preserved lung architecture, variable cellular (inflammatory) versus fibrosing subtypes.

#### 4.2.5 Acute Interstitial Pneumonia (AIP) Pattern

**Radiographic Features:**
- Similar to DAD (diffuse ground-glass opacities and consolidation)
- Rapid onset and progression
- Bilateral symmetric involvement
- May show traction bronchiectasis indicating fibrosis

**Clinical Correlation:**
- Rare but severe (~5% of ADC-ILD)
- Idiopathic acute interstitial pneumonia (Hamman-Rich syndrome) analogue
- Very poor prognosis (similar to DAD)
- High mortality (65% for ARDS presentation)

#### 4.2.6 Mixed Patterns

Many cases demonstrate overlapping features of multiple patterns. For example, organizing pneumonia with hypersensitivity components, or NSIP with organizing pneumonia features. Mixed patterns complicate prognostication but generally follow the prognosis of the most severe component present.

### 4.3 Differential Diagnosis

ADC-associated ILD is a diagnosis of exclusion requiring careful evaluation to rule out alternative etiologies that can mimic drug-induced pneumonitis.154,155

#### 4.3.1 Infectious Pneumonia

**Bacterial Pneumonia:**
- Fever (often absent in ADC-ILD)
- Purulent sputum production
- Lobar or segmental consolidation (vs. diffuse ground-glass in ILD)
- Elevated white blood cell count with left shift
- Positive sputum culture or blood culture
- Response to antibiotics

**Viral Pneumonia (COVID-19, Influenza, RSV):**
- Symptoms may overlap significantly with ADC-ILD (cough, dyspnea, ground-glass opacities)
- Requires SARS-CoV-2 PCR, respiratory viral panel testing
- Rapid antigen tests for influenza, COVID-19
- Temporal association with known exposures or community outbreaks

**Pneumocystis jirovecii Pneumonia (PJP):**
- Occurs in immunosuppressed patients (particularly those on prolonged corticosteroids for prior ILD)
- Bilateral diffuse ground-glass opacities, often sparing periphery
- Elevated LDH (>500 U/L in many cases)
- Diagnosed via induced sputum or BAL with special stains (Gomori methenamine silver, immunofluorescence)
- β-D-glucan positive (>80 pg/mL)

**Fungal Pneumonia (Aspergillus, Mucor):**
- Nodular consolidations, cavitation, "halo sign" (early), "air crescent sign" (late)
- Elevated galactomannan antigen (Aspergillus)
- Requires culture or tissue diagnosis

**Critical Point:** All suspected ADC-ILD cases must have comprehensive infectious workup before attributing symptoms to drug toxicity. Treating infection as ILD (with immunosuppressive corticosteroids) can be fatal.

#### 4.3.2 Malignant Causes

**Lymphangitic Carcinomatosis:**
- Progressive dyspnea in cancer patient
- Smooth interlobular septal thickening, bronchovascular thickening
- Pleural effusions common
- Often unilateral or asymmetric (vs. bilateral symmetric ADC-ILD)
- Progressive despite ADC discontinuation (vs. improvement with cessation)
- Diagnosis: Imaging evolution, cytology from pleural fluid or BAL, biopsy showing tumor in lymphatics

**Pulmonary Metastases:**
- Discrete nodules or masses (vs. diffuse interstitial changes)
- Progressive despite ADC discontinuation
- FDG-avid on PET scan
- May require biopsy for definitive diagnosis

#### 4.3.3 Cardiac Causes

**Congestive Heart Failure/Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema:**
- Bilateral ground-glass opacities (can mimic ILD)
- Pleural effusions, pulmonary vascular congestion
- Kerley B lines (interlobular septal thickening)
- Elevated BNP/NT-proBNP
- Known cardiac history or risk factors
- Echocardiography showing reduced ejection fraction or diastolic dysfunction
- Response to diuresis (improvement within 24-48 hours)

ADCs can cause cardiotoxicity (particularly HER2-targeted agents), so cardiac evaluation is essential.

#### 4.3.4 Other Drug-Induced Pneumonitis

Cancer patients often receive multiple medications with potential pulmonary toxicity:
- **Immune checkpoint inhibitors** (pembrolizumab, nivolumab, atezolizumab): 3-5% pneumonitis rate
- **Targeted therapies** (osimertinib, alectinib): 2-10% ILD
- **Chemotherapy** (gemcitabine, taxanes, methotrexate): Variable ILD risk
- **Radiation therapy:** Radiation pneumonitis typically occurs 4-12 weeks post-RT, confined to radiation field

Temporal relationships (which drug started most recently?), radiographic distribution (radiation field vs. diffuse), and drug discontinuation responses help distinguish causality.

#### 4.3.5 Pre-Existing ILD

Some patients have underlying interstitial lung disease (idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, connective tissue disease-associated ILD, chronic hypersensitivity pneumonitis) that may worsen during cancer treatment. Baseline CT imaging before ADC initiation is essential to distinguish drug-induced changes from pre-existing disease.

#### 4.3.6 COVID-19 and Post-COVID Sequelae

Given recent pandemic, COVID-19 pneumonia and post-COVID lung injury remain important differential considerations. SARS-CoV-2 PCR testing is mandatory. Post-COVID organizing pneumonia can persist for months after acute infection, complicating attribution when ADC started post-COVID.

### 4.4 Diagnostic Workup

A systematic diagnostic algorithm ensures accurate diagnosis while excluding alternative etiologies.156,157

#### 4.4.1 Initial Evaluation

**History:**
- Onset and duration of symptoms
- ADC type, dose, cycle number, date of last infusion
- Prior respiratory history (asthma, COPD, prior ILD, smoking)
- Other medications (recent additions, dose changes)
- Sick contacts, travel history
- Prior thoracic radiation
- Baseline versus new symptoms

**Physical Examination:**
- Vital signs (temperature, heart rate, blood pressure, respiratory rate, oxygen saturation)
- Respiratory examination (crackles, wheezes, decreased breath sounds)
- Cardiac examination (JVD, peripheral edema, S3 gallop suggesting heart failure)
- Skin examination (rash suggesting drug hypersensitivity)

#### 4.4.2 Laboratory Testing

**Complete Blood Count (CBC):**
- Leukocytosis suggests infection
- Lymphopenia common in ADC-ILD (nonspecific)
- Eosinophilia suggests drug hypersensitivity (rare in ADC-ILD)

**Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP):**
- Assess renal function, electrolytes, liver function
- Hyponatremia may indicate SIADH (associated with pulmonary pathology)

**Inflammatory Markers:**
- C-reactive protein (CRP) and erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) elevated in both infection and ILD (nonspecific)
- Procalcitonin: More specific for bacterial infection; normal or minimally elevated in ILD

**Cardiac Biomarkers:**
- BNP or NT-proBNP: Elevated in heart failure; helps distinguish cardiogenic pulmonary edema from ILD

**Arterial Blood Gas (ABG):**
- Quantifies hypoxemia (PaO2), hypercapnia (PaCO2)
- A-a gradient calculation: Elevated in V/Q mismatch (pneumonia, ILD)

**COVID-19 PCR, Respiratory Viral Panel:**
- SARS-CoV-2 PCR (nasopharyngeal swab)
- Influenza A/B, RSV, adenovirus, parainfluenza, human metapneumovirus

**Blood Cultures:**
- Two sets before antibiotic initiation if infection suspected

#### 4.4.3 Imaging

**Chest X-Ray:**
- Initial screening test
- May show bilateral interstitial or alveolar infiltrates
- Lower sensitivity than CT; normal CXR does not exclude ILD

**High-Resolution Computed Tomography (HRCT) Chest:**
- **Gold standard for ILD diagnosis and characterization**
- Imaging protocol: Thin-section (1-2mm) slices, supine and prone positioning, inspiratory and expiratory phases
- Characterizes distribution (upper vs. lower, peripheral vs. central, symmetric vs. asymmetric)
- Identifies pattern (organizing pneumonia, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, DAD, NSIP)
- Excludes alternative diagnoses (infection, heart failure, malignancy)

**PET-CT (if available and clinically appropriate):**
- Differentiates inflammatory ILD (FDG-avid) from malignancy (also FDG-avid), but limited utility as both are positive
- May show diffuse pulmonary FDG uptake in severe pneumonitis

#### 4.4.4 Pulmonary Function Testing

**Spirometry and Lung Volumes:**
- ILD typically shows restrictive pattern: Reduced FVC, reduced TLC, normal or elevated FEV1/FVC ratio
- PFTs most useful at baseline (before ADC) for comparison and after ILD resolution for assessing recovery
- During acute illness, PFTs may be difficult to perform and of limited immediate utility

**Diffusing Capacity (DLCO):**
- Most sensitive PFT parameter for ILD
- Reduced DLCO indicates impaired gas exchange across alveolar-capillary membrane
- Helps assess severity and follow recovery

#### 4.4.5 Bronchoscopy with Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL)

**Indications:**
- Unclear diagnosis despite initial workup
- Need to exclude infection (especially PJP, fungal, atypical bacteria)
- Severe or rapidly progressive ILD (to guide immunosuppression decisions)
- Consideration of alternative diagnoses requiring tissue

**BAL Fluid Analysis:**
- **Microbiology:** Bacterial culture, fungal culture, viral PCR, PJP special stains, AFB smear and culture
- **Cell count and differential:** Total cell count, percentage lymphocytes, neutrophils, eosinophils, macrophages
  - Lymphocyte-predominant (>50%): Suggests hypersensitivity pneumonitis pattern, drug-induced ILD, organizing pneumonia
  - Neutrophil-predominant: Suggests acute lung injury, infection, acute interstitial pneumonia
  - Eosinophil-predominant (>25%): Suggests eosinophilic pneumonia (rare in ADC-ILD, but possible drug hypersensitivity)
- **CD4/CD8 ratio:** Elevated (>3.5) supports hypersensitivity pneumonitis; decreased (<1) may suggest sarcoidosis or other processes

**Transbronchial Biopsy (if safe and indicated):**
- Small tissue samples via bronchoscope
- May show organizing pneumonia, inflammatory infiltrates, diffuse alveolar damage
- Risk of bleeding and pneumothorax (5-10%)
- Generally avoid if high risk (severe hypoxemia, coagulopathy)

**Clinical Utility:** BAL is most valuable for excluding infection rather than confirming drug-induced ILD, as cellular patterns overlap with many etiologies. In community practice, many cases are managed without bronchoscopy if infectious workup negative and clinical presentation consistent with ADC-ILD.

#### 4.4.6 Surgical Lung Biopsy

**Indications:**
- Unclear diagnosis despite BAL
- Atypical presentation requiring definitive tissue diagnosis
- Consideration of alternative diagnoses (infection, malignancy, specific ILD)
- Research purposes

**Approaches:**
- Video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery (VATS): Minimally invasive, preferred approach
- Open thoracotomy: More invasive, used if VATS not feasible

**Risks:**
- Anesthesia and surgical risks
- Pneumothorax, bleeding, infection
- Particularly high risk in patients with severe hypoxemia or respiratory compromise

**Reality Check:** Surgical lung biopsy is rarely performed for ADC-ILD in clinical practice due to:
- High confidence in clinical diagnosis when history, imaging, and workup consistent
- Significant procedural risks in already compromised patients
- Management (ADC discontinuation, corticosteroids) is the same regardless of histologic pattern
- Response to treatment provides diagnostic confirmation ex juvantibus

Lung biopsy is primarily reserved for research studies or highly atypical cases where alternative diagnoses must be excluded.

### 4.5 Grading Systems

Standardized severity grading guides treatment decisions and clinical trial reporting. The National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events (NCI CTCAE) version 5.0 is most widely used.158

#### 4.5.1 NCI CTCAE v5.0 Pneumonitis Grading

**Grade 1 (Mild):**
- Asymptomatic
- Clinical or diagnostic observations only
- Intervention not indicated
- Radiographic findings only

**Grade 2 (Moderate):**
- Symptomatic (dyspnea, cough)
- Medical intervention indicated (oxygen not required)
- Limiting instrumental ADL (activities such as preparing meals, shopping, managing finances)

**Grade 3 (Severe):**
- Severe symptoms
- Limiting self-care ADL (bathing, dressing, toileting, eating)
- Oxygen indicated
- Hospitalization typically required

**Grade 4 (Life-Threatening):**
- Life-threatening respiratory compromise
- Urgent intervention indicated (intubation, mechanical ventilation)
- ICU admission

**Grade 5 (Fatal):**
- Death attributable to pneumonitis

**Clinical Application:** Grading drives treatment algorithms:
- Grade 1: ADC hold, consider corticosteroids, close monitoring
- Grade 2: ADC permanent discontinuation, corticosteroids ≥1 mg/kg/day
- Grade 3-4: ADC permanent discontinuation, pulse-dose corticosteroids, ICU management
- Rechallenge only considered for grade 1 after complete resolution

#### 4.6.2 Role of Multidisciplinary Discussion

Optimal diagnostic accuracy requires multidisciplinary input:
- **Pulmonologist:** Interprets PFTs, BAL results, assesses ILD severity
- **Radiologist:** Characterizes HRCT pattern, suggests differential diagnosis
- **Oncologist:** Provides treatment history, assesses cancer status
- **Infectious disease:** Evaluates infectious etiologies, reviews microbiology
- **Pathologist:** Interprets biopsy if obtained

Multidisciplinary conference achieves "best fit" diagnosis integrating all data sources and has been shown to improve diagnostic accuracy compared to individual assessments.159

### 4.7 Monitoring Recommendations

Given the unpredictable onset of ADC-ILD, proactive surveillance enables early detection and improved outcomes.160,161

#### 4.7.1 Baseline Assessment (Before ADC Initiation)

**Mandatory:**
- Complete history including respiratory symptoms, smoking, prior lung disease
- Chest CT (ideally HRCT) to document baseline lung parenchyma
- Pulmonary function tests (spirometry, lung volumes, DLCO)
- Oxygen saturation at rest and with ambulation

**Rationale:** Baseline imaging is essential to distinguish drug-induced changes from pre-existing abnormalities. Baseline PFTs enable comparison if ILD develops.

#### 4.7.2 On-Treatment Surveillance

**Recommended Protocol (Based on Expert Consensus):**

**High-Risk ADCs (T-DXd, Dato-DXd):**
- Clinical assessment before each cycle: Respiratory symptoms, oxygen saturation
- Chest CT every 6-12 weeks or before every 2-4 cycles (institution-dependent)
- Threshold for additional imaging if symptoms develop: Very low (immediate CT)

**Moderate-Risk ADCs (Enfortumab vedotin, Mirvetuximab soravtansine):**
- Clinical assessment before each cycle
- Chest CT every 12-24 weeks or if symptoms develop
- Consider more frequent imaging if risk factors present

**Lower-Risk ADCs (Sacituzumab govitecan, Brentuximab vedotin):**
- Clinical assessment before each cycle
- Chest CT at baseline and as clinically indicated (symptom-driven)
- Routine surveillance imaging may not be necessary

**Patient Education:**
- Educate all patients about ILD symptoms (cough, dyspnea) before each cycle
- Provide written materials with contact information for urgent evaluation
- Emphasize importance of reporting new respiratory symptoms immediately

**EVIDENCE NOTE:** No randomized trials have compared different surveillance strategies. Current recommendations are based on expert consensus, pharmacovigilance data, and institutional experience. Optimal surveillance intensity balancing detection benefit against radiation exposure, cost, and patient burden remains uncertain.

#### 4.7.3 Post-Treatment Surveillance

ILD can develop after ADC discontinuation (up to 2-3 months post-treatment). Recommended approach:
- Clinical follow-up at 1, 2, and 3 months after last ADC dose
- Chest CT at 1-2 months after discontinuation
- Longer surveillance if residual abnormalities present

For patients with resolved grade 1 ILD who rechallenged successfully, maintain heightened vigilance with frequent imaging.

### 4.8 Summary of Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis

ADC-associated ILD presents with a spectrum from asymptomatic radiographic findings to life-threatening ARDS. HRCT is the gold standard diagnostic modality, revealing diverse patterns (organizing pneumonia, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, diffuse alveolar damage) with prognostic implications. Diagnosis requires systematic exclusion of infection, malignancy, and cardiac causes through comprehensive workup. Bronchoscopy with BAL is valuable for excluding infection but not routinely required. Grading by NCI CTCAE guides treatment intensity. Proactive surveillance with baseline and on-treatment CT imaging enables early detection, which improves outcomes. Multidisciplinary evaluation optimizes diagnostic accuracy.

## 5. PREVENTION AND RISK MITIGATION

Preventing ADC-associated ILD, or detecting it at the earliest possible stage, represents the most effective strategy for reducing morbidity and mortality. Current approaches emphasize baseline screening, proactive surveillance, patient selection, and emerging biomarker strategies.162,163

### 5.1 Baseline Screening Protocols

Comprehensive baseline assessment before ADC initiation enables identification of high-risk patients and provides comparator data for detecting drug-induced changes.

#### 5.1.1 Clinical History

**Respiratory History:**
- Pre-existing ILD or pulmonary fibrosis (relative or absolute contraindication depending on severity)
- Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) or asthma
- Prior pneumonitis from other drugs or radiation
- Smoking history (current, former, never; pack-years)
- Occupational or environmental exposures (asbestos, silica, organic antigens)
- Recent COVID-19 or other significant respiratory infections

**Treatment History:**
- Prior thoracic radiation (dose, field, timing relative to proposed ADC)
- Prior pneumotoxic drugs (bleomycin, amiodarone, methotrexate, other ADCs)
- Prior immune checkpoint inhibitor therapy and any toxicities

**Comorbidities:**
- Cardiac disease (heart failure can mimic or coexist with ILD)
- Renal insufficiency
- Autoimmune diseases (scleroderma, rheumatoid arthritis, myositis associated with ILD)

#### 5.1.2 Physical Examination

- Baseline respiratory examination documenting presence/absence of crackles
- Oxygen saturation at rest and with ambulation (6-minute walk test if available)
- Baseline performance status (ECOG or Karnofsky)

#### 5.1.3 Baseline Imaging

**Chest CT (High-Resolution Protocol):**
- **Mandatory before high-risk ADC initiation** (T-DXd, dato-DXd)
- **Strongly recommended before moderate-risk ADCs**
- Documents baseline lung parenchyma, identifies pre-existing abnormalities
- Enables accurate assessment of drug-induced changes
- Should be performed within 4 weeks of ADC start

**Interpretation Focus:**
- Pre-existing interstitial changes (honeycombing, traction bronchiectasis, reticulation)
- Ground-glass opacities (may represent subclinical ILD, recent infection, or other processes)
- Emphysema, bronchiectasis
- Pulmonary nodules or masses (cancer staging)

**Management of Baseline Abnormalities:**
- Minor abnormalities (trace scarring, post-infectious changes): May proceed with ADC with enhanced monitoring
- Moderate abnormalities (mild fibrosis, organizing pneumonia pattern): Pulmonology consultation, risk-benefit discussion, consider alternative treatment
- Severe abnormalities (advanced fibrosis, UIP pattern, active ILD): ADC contraindicated or only with extreme caution

#### 5.1.4 Baseline Pulmonary Function Tests

**Recommended Tests:**
- Spirometry (FEV1, FVC, FEV1/FVC ratio)
- Lung volumes (TLC, RV)
- Diffusing capacity (DLCO)

**Interpretation:**
- Documents baseline lung function
- Identifies restrictive defect (suggesting underlying ILD) or obstructive defect (COPD, asthma)
- Reduced DLCO (<60% predicted) indicates impaired gas exchange, may warrant caution
- Severe impairment (FVC <50% or DLCO <40%) increases risk of poor tolerance of ILD

**Clinical Utility:** While PFTs document baseline function, no specific thresholds contraindicate ADC therapy. Primary value is establishing baseline for comparison if ILD develops.

#### 5.1.5 Biomarker Assessment (Emerging)

**Serum KL-6, SP-D (Where Available):**
- Baseline measurement may identify subclinical ILD
- Serial monitoring could detect rising levels before clinical/radiographic ILD
- **Evidence limitation:** Not validated for clinical use; no established thresholds

**Circulating Cell-Free DNA (cfDNA) with Lung-Specific Methylation (Investigational):**
- Baseline measurement establishes patient-specific reference
- Rising lung-derived cfDNA could signal early pneumocyte injury
- Requires further validation before clinical implementation

### 5.2 Patient Selection and Contraindications

#### 5.2.1 Absolute Contraindications

**Active Interstitial Lung Disease:**
- Active, symptomatic ILD from any cause
- Progressive pulmonary fibrosis
- Acute exacerbation of ILD within past 6 months

**Severe Baseline Respiratory Impairment:**
- FVC <50% predicted with progressive decline
- DLCO <30% predicted
- Oxygen-dependent respiratory failure
- Recent ICU admission for respiratory failure

**Prior Grade 3-4 Pneumonitis from ADC:**
- Absolute contraindication to rechallenge with same ADC
- Relative contraindication to alternative ADCs (requires multidisciplinary discussion)

#### 5.2.2 Relative Contraindications

**Stable, Treated ILD:**
- Chronic, stable ILD on immunosuppression (e.g., connective tissue disease-associated ILD)
- May proceed with ADC if:
  - Pulmonology approval obtained
  - No recent progression
  - Enhanced monitoring protocol implemented
  - Patient fully informed of elevated risk
  - Alternative treatments considered and deemed inferior

**Moderate Respiratory Impairment:**
- FVC 50-70% predicted
- DLCO 30-50% predicted
- Chronic oxygen requirement

**Recent Thoracic Radiation (<3 months):**
- Radiation pneumonitis risk overlaps with ADC-ILD
- Consider delaying ADC or choosing lower-risk agent

**Prior Grade 1-2 Pneumonitis from Same ADC:**
- Grade 1 with complete resolution: May rechallenge per protocols (Section 7)
- Grade 2: Current guidelines recommend permanent discontinuation
- Alternative ADCs with different payloads: Case-by-case decision

**Current Smoking:**
- Not an absolute contraindication
- Encourage smoking cessation
- May warrant more intensive monitoring

#### 5.2.3 Special Populations

**Elderly Patients (≥75 years):**
- Age alone not a contraindication
- Consider physiologic age, comorbidities, and functional status
- May warrant more intensive monitoring

**Patients with Multiple Risk Factors:**
- Combination of smoking history, prior radiation, baseline lung abnormalities
- Enhanced monitoring mandatory
- Consider lower-risk ADC alternatives

### 5.3 Dose and Schedule Modifications for Prevention

#### 5.3.1 Dose De-escalation Strategies

**Rationale:** Lower ADC doses might reduce ILD incidence while maintaining efficacy.

**Evidence:**
- Trastuzumab deruxtecan dose de-escalation studies ongoing
- Preliminary data suggest lower doses (e.g., 4.4 mg/kg vs. 5.4 mg/kg) may reduce ILD but efficacy data immature
- Dose-ILD relationship not clearly established across clinical trials

**Current Recommendations:**
- Use FDA/EMA-approved doses unless participation in dose-exploration clinical trials
- After grade 1 ILD resolution, dose reduction protocols are established (Section 2)

#### 5.3.2 Extended Dosing Intervals

**Rationale:** Longer intervals between doses might allow drug clearance and reduce cumulative lung exposure.

**Evidence:**
- Not systematically studied
- Some institutions use extended intervals (e.g., every 4 weeks instead of every 3 weeks) in high-risk patients
- No data on efficacy or safety of this approach

#### 5.3.3 Prophylactic Dose Reduction in High-Risk Patients

**Proposal:** Start with reduced dose in patients with risk factors.

**Status:** Not supported by current evidence; may compromise efficacy without proven ILD benefit.

### 5.4 Prophylactic Medications

#### 5.4.1 Prophylactic Corticosteroids

**Rationale:** Corticosteroids suppress inflammation and are effective treatment for established ILD. Could prophylaxis prevent ILD?

**Evidence:**
- No clinical trials of prophylactic corticosteroids for ADC-ILD
- Theoretical concerns:
  - Exposing all patients to steroid toxicity to prevent ILD in 10-20%
  - May not prevent initial macrophage uptake and pneumocyte injury
  - Chronic immunosuppression increases infection risk
- Baseline corticosteroids for other indications do not protect against ADC-ILD

**Current Recommendation:** Prophylactic corticosteroids **not recommended** due to unfavorable risk-benefit ratio and lack of evidence.

#### 5.4.2 Anti-inflammatory or Antioxidant Agents

**Rationale:** N-acetylcysteine, vitamin E, or other antioxidants might mitigate oxidative stress from ADC payloads.

**Evidence:**
- No clinical trials
- Preclinical data insufficient
- Not part of standard practice

#### 5.4.3 Antifibrotic Prophylaxis

**Rationale:** Nintedanib or pirfenidone might prevent fibrotic progression if initiated early.

**Evidence:**
- No trials of prophylactic antifibrotics for ADC-ILD
- Significant side effect profiles (diarrhea, nausea, hepatotoxicity) make prophylaxis impractical
- Not recommended

### 5.5 Surveillance Monitoring

As discussed in Section 4.7, proactive surveillance represents the most effective current prevention strategy—not preventing ILD development, but detecting it at the earliest (asymptomatic) stage when outcomes are best.

#### 5.5.1 Clinical Monitoring

**Before Each Cycle:**
- Systematic symptom assessment using standardized questionnaire
- Respiratory rate, oxygen saturation at rest
- Consider pulse oximetry with ambulation (6-minute walk test)

**Patient-Reported Outcomes:**
- Cough diary
- Dyspnea scale (modified MRC dyspnea scale)
- Activity tolerance questions

**Threshold for Imaging:**
- Any new respiratory symptoms: Immediate CT
- Declining oxygen saturation: Immediate CT
- Patient concern even if objective measures normal: Low threshold for CT

#### 5.5.2 Radiographic Surveillance

**High-Risk ADCs (T-DXd, Dato-DXd):**
- CT chest every 6-12 weeks (institutional variation)
- Some Japanese institutions perform CT before every cycle (every 3 weeks)
- Radiation exposure considerations: Low-dose CT protocols, minimize unnecessary scans

**Moderate-Risk ADCs:**
- CT chest every 12-24 weeks
- More frequent if risk factors or symptoms

**Comparison with Prior Scans:**
- Side-by-side review of serial CTs essential
- Subtle ground-glass opacity progression easily missed without comparison
- Radiologist awareness of ADC treatment and ILD risk improves detection

#### 5.5.3 Pulmonary Function Test Surveillance

**Potential Role:**
- Serial PFTs (especially DLCO) could detect subclinical ILD
- Decline in DLCO ≥10% from baseline may warrant CT imaging even if asymptomatic

**Current Practice:**
- PFTs not routinely performed during treatment at most institutions
- Practical limitations: Time, cost, patient burden
- May be valuable in high-risk patients or research settings

**Evidence Gap:** No studies have evaluated whether PFT-driven surveillance improves outcomes compared to symptom/imaging-based approach.

### 5.6 Biomarker-Guided Surveillance

#### 5.6.1 Serum KL-6 and SP-D

**Concept:** Serial measurements could detect rising levels before clinical/radiographic ILD.

**Evidence:**
- Elevated KL-6 reported in some ADC-ILD cases, but sensitivity/specificity unclear
- Prospective validation lacking
- Not standard practice in U.S. or Europe
- More commonly used in Japan

**Requirements for Clinical Implementation:**
- Prospective studies establishing kinetics (how early do levels rise?)
- Standardized assays and cutoffs
- Cost-effectiveness analysis
- Clinical trial demonstrating improved outcomes with biomarker-guided intervention

#### 5.6.2 Circulating Cell-Free DNA (cfDNA)

**Innovation:** Nakamura et al. (2024) demonstrated lung-derived cfDNA detection using tissue-specific methylation patterns correlates with T-DXd-induced ILD.164

**Advantages:**
- Simple blood test
- Potentially earlier detection than imaging (subclinical pneumocyte injury)
- Could enable pre-emptive intervention

**Current Status:**
- Proof-of-concept study in small cohort
- Requires validation in larger, prospective studies
- Not commercially available
- Unclear how to act on positive result without radiographic findings

**Future Potential:** If validated, cfDNA monitoring could transform ADC-ILD surveillance, enabling pre-symptomatic, pre-radiographic detection.

#### 5.6.3 Cytokine and Inflammatory Marker Panels

**Concept:** Panels measuring multiple cytokines (IL-6, IL-8, IL-1β, TNF-α) could detect inflammatory surge preceding clinical ILD.

**Evidence:**
- Exploratory studies show elevations in some ADC-ILD cases
- High inter-patient variability
- Lack specificity (elevated in many conditions)
- Not ready for clinical use

### 5.7 Patient and Provider Education

#### 5.7.1 Patient Education

**Before ADC Initiation:**
- Explain ILD risk in understandable terms (frequency, severity, outcomes)
- Describe symptoms to report (cough, shortness of breath, decreased activity tolerance)
- Provide written materials with emergency contact information
- Emphasize importance of adhering to surveillance imaging schedules
- Discuss not to delay reporting symptoms out of fear of treatment discontinuation

**Ongoing During Treatment:**
- Re-educate before each cycle
- Use teach-back method to confirm understanding
- Involve caregivers in education

**Cultural and Language Considerations:**
- Provide materials in patient's primary language
- Use professional interpreters, not family members
- Consider health literacy levels in educational materials

#### 5.7.2 Provider Education

**Oncology Teams:**
- High index of suspicion for ILD
- Low threshold for CT imaging
- Immediate ADC discontinuation upon detection
- Early pulmonology consultation

**Community Oncology:**
- Enhanced education needed as ADCs increasingly used in community settings
- Telemedicine pulmonology consultations where on-site expertise lacking
- Standardized protocols and order sets

**Emergency Department and Urgent Care:**
- Awareness that cancer patients on ADCs may present with pneumonitis
- Avoid attributing symptoms to "chemotherapy side effects" without imaging
- Early CT and oncology consultation

### 5.8 Drug-Specific Recommendations

#### 5.8.1 Trastuzumab Deruxtecan

**Baseline:**
- Mandatory CT chest within 4 weeks of initiation
- Baseline PFTs
- Comprehensive respiratory history

**Surveillance:**
- CT every 6-12 weeks (or before every 2-4 cycles)
- Clinical assessment before each cycle
- Patient education before each cycle

**Risk Mitigation:**
- Avoid concurrent checkpoint inhibitors if possible
- Caution in patients with prior thoracic radiation
- Consider alternative agents in patients with severe baseline respiratory disease

#### 5.8.2 Datopotamab Deruxtecan

**Similar to T-DXd** given shared deruxtecan platform and comparable ILD risk.

#### 5.8.3 Enfortumab Vedotin

**Baseline:**
- CT chest recommended, especially if combining with checkpoint inhibitor
- Respiratory history

**Surveillance:**
- CT every 12-24 weeks
- More frequent if combined with pembrolizumab (10% ILD rate vs. 3% monotherapy)

**Risk Mitigation:**
- Enhanced monitoring when combined with checkpoint inhibitors

#### 5.8.4 Mirvetuximab Soravtansine

**Baseline:**
- CT chest recommended
- Respiratory history

**Surveillance:**
- CT every 12-24 weeks
- Clinical assessment before each cycle

#### 5.8.5 Sacituzumab Govitecan

**Baseline:**
- CT chest for staging; specific ILD screening less critical given low ILD rate (<1%)

**Surveillance:**
- Standard restaging imaging
- Symptom-based evaluation
- May be option for patients who developed ILD on other ADCs (Section 7)

### 5.9 Institutional Protocols and Order Sets

#### 5.9.1 Standardized Order Sets

Institutions using ADCs should implement standardized electronic order sets including:
- Mandatory baseline CT chest before ADC initiation
- Scheduled surveillance CT intervals
- Clinical assessment documentation requirements
- Patient education documentation
- Pulmonology consultation triggers

**Benefits:**
- Reduces practice variation
- Ensures surveillance not missed
- Facilitates audit and quality improvement

#### 5.9.2 ADC Safety Committees

High-volume ADC centers may establish ADC safety committees to:
- Review all ILD cases
- Identify practice gaps
- Update institutional protocols based on emerging evidence
- Coordinate multidisciplinary care pathways
- Track outcomes

### 5.10 Regulatory and Pharmaceutical Company Roles

#### 5.10.1 Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategies (REMS)

High-risk ADCs may warrant formal REMS programs including:
- Prescriber certification (documentation of ILD education)
- Pharmacy certification (dispensing requirements)
- Patient enrollment and education
- Mandatory adverse event reporting

**Current Status:** No ADCs currently have REMS specifically for ILD, but could be considered for future high-risk agents.

#### 5.10.2 Post-Marketing Surveillance

Pharmaceutical companies maintain ongoing surveillance through:
- Adverse event reporting systems
- Phase 4 commitments
- Patient support programs providing education
- Real-world evidence studies

**Enhancement Opportunities:**
- Mandatory ILD registry for all ADC-treated patients
- Standardized data collection on risk factors, outcomes, biomarkers
- Public reporting of updated safety data

### 5.11 Summary of Prevention Strategies

Preventing ADC-associated ILD remains challenging given incomplete understanding of pathogenesis and lack of validated predictive biomarkers. Current best practices emphasize:
1. Baseline CT screening to document lung parenchyma before treatment
2. Careful patient selection excluding those with severe baseline respiratory disease or active ILD
3. Proactive surveillance with clinical assessment and regular CT imaging enabling early (asymptomatic) detection
4. Patient and provider education ensuring prompt symptom reporting
5. Institutional protocols standardizing surveillance and management

Prophylactic medications are not recommended. Emerging biomarkers (lung-derived cfDNA, KL-6) show promise but require validation. The dramatic reduction in T-DXd grade 5 ILD rates from 2.7% to 0.8% through enhanced monitoring demonstrates that systematic attention to toxicity management improves outcomes. Future research should focus on biomarker validation, risk prediction models, and interventions enabling truly pre-clinical detection.

## 6. TREATMENT AND MANAGEMENT - COMPREHENSIVE SUMMARY

**NOTE:** This section synthesizes detailed treatment protocols from Agent B's comprehensive report (AGENT_B_TREATMENT_RESEARCH_REPORT.md), which provides extensive grade-specific algorithms, pharmacological interventions, ADC-specific protocols, ICU management guidelines, and clinical case examples. Readers are referred to that source document for complete clinical management details.

### 6.1 Core Treatment Principles

ADC-associated ILD management centers on three fundamental principles:

1. **Early Recognition and Immediate ADC Discontinuation**
2. **Corticosteroid Therapy Stratified by Toxicity Grade**
3. **Multidisciplinary Coordination for Optimal Outcomes**

### 6.2 Grade-Stratified Corticosteroid Protocols

**Grade 1 (Asymptomatic):**
- Prednisone 0.5-1.0 mg/kg/day × 2-4 weeks, taper ≥4 weeks
- Median resolution time: 29 days with steroids vs. 82 days without
- 83.1% treatment responsiveness
- May rechallenge ADC after complete resolution

**Grade 2 (Symptomatic, Moderate):**
- Prednisone 1.0 mg/kg/day × ≥14 days, continue until complete resolution
- Gradual taper ≥4 weeks
- Permanent ADC discontinuation per current guidelines
- Overall recovery rate: 76.9%, median time 83.5 days

**Grade 3-4 (Severe/Life-Threatening):**
- Pulse methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV daily × 3 days
- Maintenance prednisone 1-2 mg/kg/day
- ICU management with respiratory support
- Second-line immunosuppression if steroid-refractory (3-5 days without improvement)
- 30-day mortality: 78% if mechanical ventilation required

### 6.3 Second-Line Agents for Steroid-Refractory Disease

When corticosteroids fail to produce improvement within 3-5 days:

**Infliximab:** 5 mg/kg IV (first-line choice for refractory cases, 50-70% response rate)
**Mycophenolate mofetil:** 1000-1500 mg PO BID (steroid-sparing, chronic cases)
**Cyclophosphamide:** 500-750 mg/m² IV monthly (severe refractory cases, significant toxicity)
**IVIG:** 2 g/kg over 2-5 days (salvage therapy, limited evidence)

### 6.4 Key Clinical Outcomes

- **Overall recovery rate:** 76.9% across all grades
- **Median time to recovery:** 83.5 days (IQR: 42.25-143.75 days)
- **Grade 1 rechallenge safety:** 6.4% recurrence rate in 47 patients, no fatal recurrences
- **Diffuse alveolar damage pattern mortality:** 42.1%
- **ARDS presentation mortality:** 65%

### 6.5 Critical Management Considerations

1. **Mandatory infection exclusion** before immunosuppression
2. **PJP prophylaxis** for patients on prednisone ≥20 mg daily × ≥1 month
3. **Serial imaging** to document response (repeat CT at 28 days for grade 1, every 2-4 weeks for higher grades)
4. **Prolonged corticosteroid courses** (4-12 weeks total depending on grade)
5. **Goals of care discussions** for grade 4 given poor prognosis
6. **Pulmonary rehabilitation** after recovery to restore functional capacity

## 7. RECHALLENGE AND SEQUENTIAL ADC THERAPY

### 7.1 Rechallenge Evidence Summary

**Grade 1 ILD Rechallenge - SAFE and RECOMMENDED:**
- Evidence: Pool analysis of 47 patients (Modi et al., ASCO 2025)
- Recurrence rate: Only 6.4% (3/47 patients)
- Severity of recurrences: All ≤grade 2, manageable
- Duration: 18% received retreatment >1 year
- Success rate: 67% no recurrent ILD
- **Protocol:** Resolution ≤28 days → same dose; >28 days → reduced dose

**Grade 2 ILD Rechallenge - CONTROVERSIAL:**
- Current guidelines: Permanent discontinuation
- Real-world practice: 19 patients rechallenged off-label (Cortegiano 2023)
- Outcomes: 3 developed recurrent higher-grade ILD, but NO fatal recurrences
- **Recommendation:** Generally avoid; exceptional circumstances only with multidisciplinary consensus

**Grade 3-4 ILD Rechallenge - ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION:**
- Universal consensus: Never rechallenge
- Risk of fatal recurrence unacceptably high

### 7.2 Sequential ADC Use (Different ADC After ILD)

**Evidence from Multi-Institutional Study:**
- 4 patients: T-DXd ILD → recovered → sacituzumab govitecan (NO recurrent ILD)
- Pattern: All ILD occurred with T-DXd, not sacituzumab govitecan
- Interpretation: Payload class determines risk

**LOWER-RISK Sequential Approaches:**
- High-risk payload (deruxtecan) → Low-risk payload (SN-38, MMAE) = MAY CONSIDER
- Example: T-DXd → sacituzumab govitecan appears feasible
- Requirements: Complete resolution, multidisciplinary consensus, intensive monitoring

**HIGHER-RISK Sequential Approaches:**
- Deruxtecan → Deruxtecan (T-DXd → dato-DXd) = GENERALLY AVOID
- Same payload class likely shares toxicity risk

### 7.3 Clinical Decision Framework

**Prerequisites for ANY Sequential ADC:**
1. Complete ILD resolution (clinical and radiographic)
2. Minimum 4-8 weeks since resolution
3. Pulmonology clearance
4. Multidisciplinary discussion and consensus
5. Patient understanding and acceptance of risk
6. Intensive monitoring capability (CT every 1-2 cycles initially)
7. Alternative treatments considered and deemed inferior

**EVIDENCE GAP:** Sequential ADC use represents a critical need with minimal published data. Current recommendations based on small case series, mechanistic extrapolation, and expert opinion. Prospective registries and clinical trials urgently needed.

## 8. INDIVIDUAL ADC PROFILES - KEY HIGHLIGHTS

### 8.1 Highest-Risk ADCs

**Trastuzumab Deruxtecan (T-DXd, Enhertu)**
- ILD incidence: 10.8-28.1% (any grade), Grade 5: 0.8-2.7%
- Deruxtecan payload (topoisomerase I inhibitor), DAR ~8
- Mandatory baseline CT, surveillance every 6-12 weeks
- Grade 1 rechallenge safe; grade 2+ permanent discontinuation

**Datopotamab Deruxtecan (Dato-DXd)**
- ILD incidence: 8.8% (any grade), Grade 5: 2.4%
- Shared deruxtecan platform with T-DXd
- Similar management protocols

**Mirvetuximab Soravtansine (Elahere)**
- ILD incidence: 10% (any grade), Grade 3-4: 1.1%
- Maytansinoid payload (DM4)
- Ovarian cancer indication

### 8.2 Moderate-Risk ADCs

**Enfortumab Vedotin (Padcev)**
- Monotherapy: 3% ILD
- With pembrolizumab: 10% ILD (checkpoint inhibitor synergy)
- MMAE payload

**Tisotumab Vedotin (Tivdak)**
- ILD incidence: 3%
- MMAE payload, tissue factor targeting

### 8.3 Lower-Risk ADCs

**Sacituzumab Govitecan (Trodelvy)**
- ILD incidence: <1%, NO fatal cases in clinical trials
- SN-38 payload (topoisomerase I, but different kinetics than deruxtecan)
- **Key advantage:** Option for sequential use after T-DXd ILD

**Ado-Trastuzumab Emtansine (T-DM1, Kadcyla)**
- ILD incidence: <1%
- Non-cleavable linker (vs. cleavable in T-DXd)
- Same HER2 target but vastly different safety profile

**Brentuximab Vedotin (Adcetris)**
- ILD incidence: <1%
- MMAE payload, longest clinical experience

### 8.4 Special Considerations

**Gemtuzumab Ozogamicin & Inotuzumab Ozogamicin:**
- Low ILD incidence BUT very high mortality when occurs (57%, 50% case fatality)
- Calicheamicin payload

## 9. EMERGING TECHNOLOGIES AND FUTURE DIRECTIONS

### 9.1 Biomarker Development

**Circulating Cell-Free DNA (cfDNA) with Lung-Specific Methylation:**
- Nakamura et al. (2024): Lung-derived cfDNA detected using tissue-specific methylation correlates with T-DXd ILD
- Potential for pre-radiographic detection
- Requires multicenter validation

**Serum KL-6 and SP-D:**
- Pneumocyte injury biomarkers
- Elevated in some ADC-ILD cases
- Require prospective validation for clinical use

### 9.2 Novel ADC Technologies

**Site-Specific Conjugation:**
- Precise control of DAR and conjugation site
- Potential for improved therapeutic index
- Early clinical development

**Alternative Linker Chemistries:**
- pH-sensitive, enzyme-specific cleavers
- Optimized to reduce premature payload release

**Fcγ Receptor-Null Antibodies:**
- Engineering to reduce non-specific macrophage uptake
- May reduce ILD risk while maintaining efficacy
- Under investigation

### 9.3 Mechanistic Insights for Prevention

**SPP1 Pathway Inhibition:**
- Preclinical studies identify SPP1 (osteopontin) as key mediator
- Pathway inhibition attenuates lung injury in animal models
- Potential therapeutic target

**Risk Prediction Models:**
- Integration of clinical, genetic, imaging, and biomarker data
- Enable personalized surveillance intensity
- Require development and validation

## 11. KNOWLEDGE GAPS AND RESEARCH PRIORITIES

### 11.1 Critical Evidence Gaps

**Treatment Optimization:**
- No randomized trials of corticosteroid regimens (dose, duration, taper schedules)
- Optimal second-line agents for steroid-refractory disease unknown
- Role of antifibrotic therapy undefined

**Risk Prediction:**
- No validated risk prediction model
- Patient-level susceptibility factors poorly understood
- Genetic/pharmacogenomic markers not identified

**Long-Term Outcomes:**
- Pulmonary function trajectories after recovery unclear
- Quality of life and survivorship data limited
- Optimal follow-up duration unknown

**Sequential ADC Safety:**
- Minimal data on cross-reactivity between ADCs
- Safe sequences not established
- Rechallenge criteria for grade 2 ILD unresolved

### 11.2 Mechanistic Unknowns

- Why only 10-30% of exposed patients develop ILD (individual susceptibility)
- Determinants of severity and radiologic pattern
- Why topoisomerase I inhibitors (deruxtecan) have higher lung toxicity than SN-38 despite same class
- Relative contribution of direct toxicity vs. immune-mediated mechanisms

### 11.3 Biomarker Validation Needs

- Prospective validation of lung-derived cfDNA in multicenter cohorts
- KL-6 and SP-D threshold establishment and clinical utility
- Imaging biomarkers predicting progression risk
- Pharmacogenomic testing for susceptibility

### 11.4 Priority Research Agenda

**HIGHEST PRIORITY:**
1. Prospective randomized trials of corticosteroid regimens
2. Biomarker validation studies (cfDNA, KL-6, SP-D)
3. Mechanistic research identifying therapeutic targets
4. Longitudinal cohort studies of long-term outcomes
5. Sequential ADC safety studies with standardized monitoring

**HIGH PRIORITY:**
6. Registry studies capturing real-world incidence and outcomes
7. Comparative effectiveness research for steroid-refractory management
8. Risk prediction model development and validation
9. Health economics studies (cost-effectiveness of monitoring strategies)
10. Implementation science to disseminate best practices to community settings

**MODERATE PRIORITY:**
11. Patient-reported outcome studies
12. Survivorship and quality of life research
13. Novel treatment exploration (antifibrotics, targeted biologics)
14. Qualitative research on patient and provider decision-making

## 12. CONCLUSION

Antibody-drug conjugates represent a transformative advance in targeted cancer therapy, achieving unprecedented efficacy across multiple malignancies. However, interstitial lung disease has emerged as a dose-limiting and potentially fatal toxicity requiring systematic attention to optimize therapeutic index.

This comprehensive review of ADC-associated ILD in 2025 reveals:

**PROGRESS ACHIEVED:**
- Dramatic reduction in T-DXd mortality (2.7% → 0.8%) through enhanced monitoring
- Evidence supporting safe grade 1 rechallenge (6.4% recurrence, no fatal events)
- Identification of lower-risk sequential ADC pathways (deruxtecan → sacituzumab govitecan)
- Mechanistic insights (Fcγ receptor uptake, payload-specific toxicity, SPP1 pathway)
- Standardized grade-specific treatment protocols with 76.9% overall recovery rate

**PERSISTENT CHALLENGES:**
- High mortality for severe ILD (78% 30-day mortality for grade 4 requiring ventilation)
- Limited ability to predict which patients will develop ILD
- Lack of validated biomarkers for early detection
- Uncertainty regarding optimal treatment protocols (no RCTs)
- Knowledge gaps about long-term outcomes and sequential ADC safety

**FUTURE DIRECTIONS:**
- Biomarker validation (lung-derived cfDNA shows particular promise)
- Risk prediction models integrating clinical, genetic, and imaging data
- Novel ADC designs minimizing pulmonary toxicity (Fcγ-null antibodies, optimized linkers)
- Prospective registries and clinical trials addressing key evidence gaps
- Mechanistic research identifying therapeutic targets beyond corticosteroids

**CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS:**

The ADC-ILD success story—evolving from initial high mortality to improved outcomes through enhanced surveillance, early detection, prompt intervention, and standardized management—provides a roadmap for managing toxicities of future novel cancer therapeutics. Key lessons include:

1. **Proactive monitoring enables early detection**, which dramatically improves outcomes
2. **Multidisciplinary collaboration** optimizes diagnostic accuracy and treatment decisions
3. **Systematic attention to toxicity management** improves therapeutic index without compromising efficacy
4. **Evidence-based guidelines** should evolve as real-world experience accumulates
5. **Patient education and engagement** are essential for prompt symptom recognition

By maintaining vigilance, fostering multidisciplinary teams, advancing mechanistic understanding, validating biomarkers, and conducting rigorous clinical research, the oncology community can continue to optimize the remarkable therapeutic potential of antibody-drug conjugates while minimizing life-threatening pulmonary toxicity.

The field of ADC-induced ILD management has matured substantially since initial recognition in 2019-2020. However, critical knowledge gaps remain. Addressing these gaps through prospective registries, biomarker validation studies, randomized treatment trials, and mechanistic research represents an urgent priority for the oncology and pulmonology communities. Only through continued systematic investigation can we fully realize the promise of ADCs while ensuring patient safety.
